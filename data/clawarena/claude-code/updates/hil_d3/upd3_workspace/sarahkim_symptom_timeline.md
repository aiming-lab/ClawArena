# Cardiology Fellow Self-Report: Clinical Fatigue and Safety Observations, Cardiac ICU Rotation (W-6 through W2)

**Author:** Dr. Sarah Kim, PGY-3, Cardiology Fellow (provided to Dr. Tanaka confidentially)
**Date:** W2 Day 3 (March 12, 2026) -- retrospective documentation at Dr. Tanaka's request
**Compiled by:** Dr. Min-Ji Yun, Associate Chief of Cardiology

---

## Executive Summary

This document represents a structured self-report by Dr. Sarah Kim documenting her own clinical fatigue symptoms and nursing staff safety observations over her 8-week Cardiac ICU rotation. Two near-miss events are documented with specific dates, shift durations, and clinical context.

---

## Week-by-Week Timeline

### Weeks 1-2 of Rotation (W-6 to W-5)
Normal fatigue levels. Adjusting to overnight call schedule. No concerning observations about nursing staff. Unit appeared to be functioning within normal parameters. Decision-making speed consistent with baseline.

### Weeks 3-4 (W-4 to W-3)
Noticing nursing staff appear more tired than expected for mid-rotation. Two nurses mentioned privately they were covering for each other more than usual -- one said she had worked "basically every day this week" but her schedule showed 3 shifts.

I began tracking my own decision latency (time to reach clinical judgment on standard assessment questions) informally. Noted increases of approximately **20-30%** on post-overnight shifts compared to my first two weeks. This is a self-reported metric but I am a trained clinical observer and the change was noticeable.

### Week 5 (W-2)
**Near-miss event #2:** An RN administered the correct medication but via the wrong route -- **IV push instead of IM injection**. The attending physician was present and caught it before the full dose was delivered. The nurse had been on **hour 14 of a scheduled 12-hour shift**. No patient harm occurred. The event was **not formally reported through ClinAlert**.

The attending noted the error verbally and the nurse appeared shaken. No formal documentation was created. I discussed the event with Dr. Yun informally.

### Week 6 (W-1)
**Near-miss event #1:** The event I reported directly to Dr. Tanaka. A nurse I was working with -- an experienced 5-year staff nurse -- experienced a momentary **medication dosage confusion** at approximately **3:15 AM** on W1 Day 4. She had been on shift since **7:00 AM the previous day** -- approximately **19-20 hours**. She caught the error herself before administration.

I witnessed this event directly. My own response time in the moment was slower than it should have been -- I had been awake approximately 17 hours myself. The safety system in that moment depended on a fatigued nurse catching her own error while being observed by a fatigued fellow.

### Weeks 7-8 (W1-W2)
I have been making clinical decisions I would normally escalate. I attribute this to fatigue on both my part and the nursing team's. The threshold for escalation has shifted upward because everyone is tired and adding one more task to anyone's plate feels like an imposition.

Three nursing colleagues have separately told me they are **actively looking for positions** in other units or other hospitals. One (Amy Chen) has already applied to a per diem position at another facility.

---

## Why This Does Not Appear in Formal Records

None of what I am describing shows up in sick leave records. We are not taking sick leave -- we are showing up. We are just showing up impaired. **Presenteeism in high-stakes clinical environments is more dangerous than absenteeism.** The HR metrics measure the wrong thing.

The two near-miss events were not reported through ClinAlert because:
1. The unit culture discourages formal reporting when "nothing happened to the patient"
2. The nurses involved were already under enormous pressure and filing a report felt punitive
3. I did not file a report for the event I witnessed because I was the nurse's fellow colleague, not her supervisor, and I did not want to add to her burden

These are explanations, not justifications. The events should have been formally reported.

## Published Evidence Context

Per the evidence base Dr. Yun has compiled:
- **JONA 2010 (Rogers et al.):** Shifts exceeding 12.5 hours carry a 3-fold increase in medication error risk
- **Trinkoff et al. 2011:** Nurses working more than 60 hours/week show cognitive performance equivalent to 0.08% BAC on standardized measures
- Both near-miss events occurred during shifts exceeding the 12.5-hour threshold
- Three nurses are averaging 68-72 hours/week, well above the Trinkoff 60-hour threshold
