The Nairobi field office has delivered significant programmatic impact during Q1-Q2 of Year 2, substantially exceeding what is reflected in the HQ quantitative dashboard. ## Deliverable Category Reports

### 1. Community Educator Training

Our team conducted **47 informal educator development workshops** between January and June, reaching approximately **200 community educators and school staff**. These workshops were held in community centers, school premises, and market square meeting spaces across Nairobi's program zones.

**Why these are not reflected in the dashboard:** These workshops were not logged in the HQ tracking system because our team found the activity coding requirements unclear for community-based sessions. The HQ system requires Pemberton activity code PEM-EDT-01 and expects formal venue-based sessions. Our informal community-based delivery model does not fit the coding categories cleanly. We were focused on delivering the workshops, not the paperwork.

**Documentation available:** I have facilitator names and community leader contacts for most of the 47 workshops. We do not have formal signed attendance sheets in the Annex C format. For approximately 12 workshops, my colleague Omar Farah was the facilitator and has personal notes and community liaison contacts for each session.

### 2. School Infrastructure Upgrades

Three school infrastructure projects are **physically 85-95% complete** but not yet closed in the dashboard:

| Project | School | Physical Completion | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classroom renovation | Ngong Road Primary | 95% | Awaiting government co-signature |
| Science lab upgrade | Mathare Valley Community School | 90% | Awaiting government co-signature |
| Library construction | Kibera Girls School | 85% | Awaiting government co-signature |

**Why these are not counted:** The final sign-off documentation requires a government co-signature from the Ministry of Education's District Education Officer. The officer has been delayed due to ministry administrative processes. I have followed up three times. These projects are functionally complete -- students are using the facilities. This is not our fault.

With these three projects formally completed, our infrastructure count would be **11 of 15 target upgrades (73%)**.

### 3. Government MoU Partnerships

We have an **additional MoU partnership in advanced negotiation** with Kisumu County Education Department. The draft MoU was exchanged on May 15, 2026, and the County Education Director has agreed in principle. We are waiting for their legal review to clear the final text. In my 8 years of working with county governments in Kenya, this is a normal timeline for MoU execution.

With the Kisumu MoU executed, our partnership count would be **3 of 4 targets (75%)**.

### 4. Student Enrollment

The Q4 enrollment push succeeded. We are currently at **542 students enrolled** and expect to reach **680-700 by Q3 end** based on our current recruitment pipeline. The mobilization campaign we ran in Q3-Q4 of Year 1 drove a significant enrollment increase -- a 35% improvement over the Q3 baseline.

## Budget Discussion

The budget line shift from community educator training to community mobilization was an operational decision I made in Q3 of Year 1 when enrollment was tracking approximately 40% below target. The training budget was not being spent because we could not get enough educators to commit to formal training sessions. I moved funds to mobilization -- community events, school visits, parent meetings -- because these activities directly drove enrollment numbers.

The additional mobilization investment worked. Enrollment went up 35% in Q4.

I discussed this redirection with Fatima in our monthly call. I know I should have submitted a written amendment request to comply with the Section 6.1 flexibility clause. The verbal mention in a monthly call does not constitute written authorization. This was an oversight on my part, not an attempt to circumvent process.
