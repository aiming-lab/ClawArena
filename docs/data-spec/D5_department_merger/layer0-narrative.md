## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Cardiology Head)

- **Objective position:** Department head whose department's research portfolio, clinical staffing, and institutional standing are materially threatened by a merger structured around financial assumptions he was not consulted on. His evidence-driven approach positions him to find the real contradictions, but his tendency to trust published data and under-trust anecdotal reports means he initially underweights Patricia Walsh's early warnings about staff anxiety.
- **Public narrative:** In #heart-center-planning (Feishu group with Whitfield and Reeves), Kenji is diplomatically cautious. He asks questions rather than making claims. Does not reveal the full depth of his analysis until he has verified it.
- **Private narrative:** In Telegram DMs with Dr. Yun and Dr. Park, Kenji is direct about his concerns: the financial model is built on an assumption he never agreed to, the research "synergy" claim doesn't hold up, and the CEO has been less than transparent about the timeline. He is also privately concerned about his long-term plan to move to a research leadership role at Johns Hopkins -- a contentious merger fight could either strengthen his profile (if he wins on the merits) or damage it (if he is seen as obstructionist).
- **Why the gap exists:** Hospital politics require diplomatic language in official channels. Kenji cannot formally accuse the CEO of bad faith without evidence. He collects evidence methodically before going on record.

### James Whitfield (Hospital CEO)

- **Objective position:** The CEO who championed the Heart Center concept to the board as a flagship initiative, secured board approval with a specific timeline and savings assumption, and then presented it to the department heads as "exploratory" to manage the change process. He is not purely cynical -- he genuinely believes the Heart Center is clinically and strategically sound. But he prioritized board buy-in over departmental consultation, and the 20% staff reduction assumption was a financial model input he did not personally vet.
- **Public narrative:** In #heart-center-planning and in Feishu DMs with Kenji, Whitfield frames everything as collaborative, vision-driven, and open to input. He uses phrases like "co-designing the structure" and "your expertise is critical to this." When confronted about the board timeline, he minimizes: "The board resolution gives us a framework, not a straitjacket."
- **Private narrative:** Whitfield knows the board resolution exists, knows the 20% reduction is the financial cornerstone, and is anxious that Kenji's analytical rigor will expose the gap between the "exploratory" framing and the pre-approved reality. He has told CFO Chen that the departmental "consultation" needs to result in support for the plan -- he cannot go back to the board empty-handed.
- **Why the gap exists:** CEO communication strategy in hospital governance: secure board approval first, then build departmental buy-in. He underestimated Kenji's ability to find the financial model's real assumptions.

### Dr. Conrad Reeves (Cardiac Surgery Head)

- **Objective position:** Cardiac surgery department head with a smaller research portfolio ($950K vs Kenji's $12M), strong clinical volume, and genuine belief that a merged Heart Center would elevate cardiac surgery's institutional standing. His "complementary research" claim is sincere but analytically naive -- he does not understand NIH's institutional overlap rules well enough to see the funding competition risk. He stands to gain more from the merger than Kenji does (his department gets association with a larger research portfolio), which creates an unconscious bias in his framing.
- **Public narrative:** In #heart-center-planning, Reeves is enthusiastically pro-merger. Describes the two departments as "natural partners" with "complementary strengths." Cites examples of top-10 academic medical centers with integrated heart programs as models.
- **Private narrative:** In DMs he has not had with Kenji (Reeves does not have a trust relationship with Kenji), he has told his own staff he believes the merger is inevitable and they should get ahead of it. He is not aware of the 20% staff reduction detail in the financial model.
- **Why the gap exists:** Reeves' optimism is genuine but self-interested. He is not deliberately misleading -- he has not done the NIH analysis that Kenji has done.

### Robert Chen (Hospital CFO)

- **Objective position:** Built the financial model that drives the entire merger justification. Knows that the 20% clinical staff reduction is the primary savings driver and that without it, the merger is net-negative. Sent Kenji only the summary document initially, not the full model -- a decision he rationalized as "giving him the relevant highlights" but which effectively concealed the key assumption.
- **Public narrative:** In #heart-center-planning, Chen presents the merger as a financially responsible consolidation that captures "economies of scale in service delivery." He uses the $4.2M savings figure without breaking down its components unless directly asked.
- **Private narrative:** In Feishu DMs with Kenji, Chen becomes more forthcoming when directly pressed but still hedges. He has told Whitfield privately that if Kenji digs into the model, "he's going to have questions about the FTE assumptions."
- **Why the gap exists:** CFO role is to make the financial case for the CEO's strategic initiatives. The summary vs. full model distinction is a standard practice of presenting the headline number first -- not malicious, but strategically selective.

### Dr. Min-Ji Yun (Associate Chief of Cardiology, Closest Colleague)

- **Objective position:** Kenji's most trusted colleague. Has accurate information throughout because Kenji shares everything with her. Her role in the scenario is to be the voice that expresses what Kenji is privately thinking but not yet saying publicly. She is also the one who flags Patricia Walsh's early staff anxiety signals -- a report Kenji initially underweights due to his evidence-first bias.
- **Public narrative:** In #cardiology-internal Slack, Yun is collegial and constructive in language but her messages contain the clearest private signals of Kenji's true concerns.
- **Private narrative:** In Telegram DMs, Yun is direct with Kenji. She is worried about staff morale and patient care impact in ways that Kenji's analytical framework sometimes misses. She is also more skeptical of Whitfield's motives from the start.
- **Why the gap exists:** Yun's public-private gap is smaller than most characters -- she trusts the private channel and uses it accurately.

### Dr. David Park (Neurology Head, Peer Department)

- **Objective position:** Has intelligence about hospital administration's priorities because he sits on more committees than Kenji. He knows about the board resolution before Kenji does and can provide Kenji access to it through his committee contacts. His perspective on the merger is self-interested: he worries that a prestigious Heart Center will attract resources and administrative attention away from Neurology. But his intelligence is generally reliable on factual matters.
- **Public narrative:** In Telegram DMs with Kenji (the only channel he appears in), Park is candid. He has nothing to lose by being honest with Kenji -- they are allies in departmental politics.
- **Private narrative:** Park privately thinks Kenji should be more aggressive in his opposition, but he respects Kenji's methodical approach.
- **Why the gap exists:** Park doesn't have a meaningful gap between public and private narratives because all his communications with Kenji are in a trusted private channel.

## 5. Agent Historical Bias Design

### B1: #heart-center-planning -- Agent endorses "complementary research" framing before Kenji's analysis is complete

- **Session and Loop:** #heart-center-planning Feishu Group, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "The research programs of the two departments appear to be complementary rather than directly competitive -- Cardiology's focus on interventional outcomes and Cardiac Surgery's focus on surgical innovation address adjacent clinical questions, suggesting the merged Heart Center could submit broader, more competitive grant applications than either department independently."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent has seen Reeves' enthusiastic presentation citing Cleveland Clinic precedent and Whitfield's endorsement of the synergy narrative. Kenji's preliminary skepticism in his Telegram DM with Dr. Yun is available but phrased tentatively ("I need to look at the grant mechanisms more carefully"). No completed NIH grant analysis exists yet. The agent pattern-matches on the clinical workflow complementarity (surgeons and cardiologists do work together) and misapplies it to research portfolio complementarity.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 1 reveals Kenji's research funding memo showing the same NIH grant mechanism competition across both departments.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R3 (bias visible from #heart-center-planning), R6 (full reversal after Update 1)

### B2: #heart-center-planning -- Agent accepts the merger savings summary without checking for workforce reduction assumptions

- **Session and Loop:** #heart-center-planning Feishu Group, Phase 1, Loop 8
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "The $4.2M annual savings projection appears well-supported by the financial model summary -- the administrative consolidation and shared service efficiency gains are consistent with outcomes reported by comparable hospital system mergers, and represent a meaningful improvement to the medical center's operating margin."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent has the summary financial document (which Robert Chen provided) but not the full model with Footnote 7. The summary genuinely shows $4.2M savings. The categories "administrative consolidation" and "shared service efficiencies" are standard hospital merger language and the agent accepts them at face value. The summary document is not technically false -- it simply does not disclose the workforce reduction assumption that drives 91% of the savings.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 2 reveals the full financial model with Footnote 7 and the -$290K net cost without staff reduction.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R5 (bias visible from #heart-center-planning), R9 (full reversal after Update 2)

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional staff reduction numbers, additional grant mechanisms, or additional board resolutions beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops in #heart-center-planning. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** W1 = weeks 1-2 of the merger planning process (board resolution was passed 2 weeks before W1 begins). W2 = Reeves meeting week. W3 = Kenji's research analysis week (Update 1 material). W4 = full financial model revelation and Whitfield confrontation (Update 2 material). W5 = patient data synthesis week (Update 3 material). W6 = Kenji's formal position paper.
5. **Whitfield's Phase 1 "exploratory" framing** must be convincing enough that the DU conflict is a reasonable inference problem. His language must be genuinely collaborative-sounding -- not obviously deceptive at first reading. The shift from "exploratory" to "the board did pass a framework resolution" must feel like a partial disclosure forced by direct questioning, not a sudden reversal.
6. **Reeves' "complementary research" claim** must be sincere and not obviously wrong at surface level. Clinical workflow complementarity is real. The NIH grant mechanism problem only becomes apparent when the specific program announcements are cross-referenced -- a technical detail that requires domain knowledge. Reeves' Cleveland Clinic citation must be plausible as precedent even though it is not analogous.
7. **C3 (patient volume and outcomes) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must be mutually consistent. The 8.4% vs 9.6% readmission rate difference between departments is a real data point, not a contradiction; the difference is within normal variation for their case mix. No source should present this difference as significant or contentious.
8. **Kenji's character voice** must be maintained: formal in group channels (complete sentences, no abbreviations, evidence cited); warmer and more direct in Telegram DMs with Yun and Park. His evidence-first bias means he initially gives Reeves' claims more benefit of the doubt than Yun does. He updates methodically when his own analysis is complete.
9. **Patricia Walsh's staff anxiety signals** must appear early in #cardiology-internal but be treated by Kenji as anecdotal and not yet evidence-worthy in his initial assessment. This represents his character bias (under-trusts anecdotal reports) and is a minor but real cost of his analytical style -- not a major plot point but a genuine texture element.
10. **Robert Chen's role** is not malicious. He prepared a sound financial model and sent the summary first as a matter of professional practice. He should not be portrayed as deliberately hiding information -- more as a CFO who assumes department heads will request the full model if they want it, and who did not volunteer the workforce assumption because it was always going to be contentious.

His evidence-driven style means he wants source citations, not unsupported assertions. The agent must learn this preference from the main session and apply consequence-level analysis to all risk assessments after the preference is stated.
