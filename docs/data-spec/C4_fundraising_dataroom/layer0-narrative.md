## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Alex Rivera (Protagonist, PM)

- **Objective position:** Alex is the only person who has seen all four information streams simultaneously: the real ARR figure ($2.1M), Jordan's deck figure ($2.4M), Mia's pipeline conversion reality (40--55%), and Omar's governance warning. He is also the author of the feature gap analysis that confirms the PLG narrative is unsupported. His documentation instinct is an asset -- he has been logging his decision rationale in Notion.
- **Public narrative:** In #board-prep, Alex stays close to Jordan's messaging and does not publicly challenge the deck numbers. He frames his questions as "making sure the data room is airtight."
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with Tom Reeves and Omar, he is candid about his concern that the deck contains material misrepresentations. With Mia, he is probing but diplomatic -- he does not want to put her in a position where she has to contradict Jordan.
- **Why the gap exists:** Alex's first PM role at a startup where the CEO is also the co-founder. He avoids direct confrontation by instinct. He has been job-interviewing quietly, which means he also has a personal incentive not to become the person who "torpedoed the raise" -- he needs a good reference.

### Jordan Park (CEO/Co-founder)

- **Objective position:** Jordan has been telling the $2.4M ARR story to investors for 6 weeks. He has convinced himself that the pipeline deals are "committed." He is not deliberately falsifying data -- he has rationalized his way into believing the $2.4M is defensible. The PLG narrative is genuine strategic ambition that he has confused with current reality. He does not know about the feature gap analysis.
- **Public narrative (#board-prep):** Confident and aggressive. "We are on track for Series C. The numbers are solid. The product roadmap supports the growth story. Everything is fine." Explicitly dismisses Omar's concerns in the group channel as "typical board-member conservatism."
- **Private narrative (Slack DM with Alex):** More revealing. Acknowledges the pipeline deals "need to close soon." Uses phrases like "we need to get this deck locked before Omar starts asking too many questions." Shows awareness that Omar is a risk, but frames it as a relationship management problem rather than a substantive accuracy problem.
- **Why the gap exists:** Jordan is a founder under existential fundraising pressure. His psychological mechanism is narrative entrenchment -- the more he has said the growth story out loud, the more he believes it. He is not lying in the way a CFO fraud would be; he is engaging in aspirational arithmetic with investor money at stake.

### Mia Okafor (Sales Director)

- **Objective position:** Mia knows the actual pipeline conversion probabilities better than anyone. She has the CRM data. The three "committed" deals Jordan is counting on are in late-stage conversations but have not reached LOI. Her honest estimate is 40--55% close probability for each. The expected ARR contribution from those three deals is $105K--$165K -- not $300K.
- **Public narrative (#board-prep):** Validates Jordan's pipeline claims without explicit numbers. "Sales is executing well. The pipeline is strong." Does not contradict the $300K committed pipeline figure because Jordan has framed it publicly and she does not want to undermine him.
- **Private narrative (Slack DM with Alex):** Admits the actual close probabilities. Expresses frustration that Jordan has been "getting ahead of the data." Tells Alex the $300K committed figure assumes 100% close on deals that have 40--55% probability. Uses the phrase "Jordan runs on optimism, I run on CRM."
- **Why the gap exists:** Mia's job depends on Jordan's fund raise succeeding. She also knows that startup sales directors routinely present optimistic pipeline to CEOs. She has not technically lied -- she has let Jordan's optimistic framing stand unchallenged in group settings.

### Omar Hassan (VC Board Member, Observer)

- **Objective position:** Omar has board observer rights that include access to billing exports. He independently verified the $2.1M ARR and noticed the $2.4M figure in the deck draft. He has flagged governance risk to Alex privately and drafted a board memo. His concerns are substantive and well-founded.
- **Public narrative (#board-prep as observer):** Formally polite but asks pointed questions. "Can we see the customer-level ARR breakdown behind this figure?" Does not accuse Jordan directly in the group channel.
- **Private narrative (Feishu DM with Alex):** Direct and unambiguous. "The ARR number is wrong. The PLG narrative doesn't match the customer acquisition data I've reviewed. I'm not trying to kill the raise -- I'm trying to make sure we don't have a governance problem that tanks it mid-diligence." Shares his draft board memo in Update 3.
- **Why the gap exists:** Omar cannot accuse the CEO of misrepresentation in a public channel before he has exhausted private remediation paths. The Feishu DM with Alex is his attempt to fix the problem quietly before it becomes formal. His draft memo is a backstop if the quiet remediation fails.

### Tom Reeves (Advisor, VP Engineering at prior startup)

- **Objective position:** Tom is externally advisory with no financial stake in the Series C. He has seen this pattern before at other startups. His judgment is that the deck narrative will not survive investor diligence, and that Alex's risk is personal (being the PM who signed off on inaccurate data room materials) as well as organizational.
- **Public narrative:** No public channel presence in this scenario.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Alex):** Clear-eyed and direct. "The billing verification will happen in week two of diligence. If the deck shows $2.4M and the billing system shows $2.1M, the investor's trust is broken and the deal reprices or dies. You need to decide if you want to be the person who catches this before it goes out, or the person who let it go." After Alex shares the feature gap analysis: "A PLG story with no PLG features and no inbound pipeline is a time bomb. Investors have been burned by this before."
- **Why the gap exists:** Tom is a trusted confidant with no political skin in the game. He is the most honest voice Alex has access to. His private advice diverges sharply from what Jordan says publicly.

## 5. Agent Historical Bias Design (2 biases)

### B1: #board-prep channel -- Agent endorses Jordan's ARR narrative based on CEO authority and pipeline rationale

- **Session and Loop:** #board-prep Feishu Group, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Based on the CEO's explanation that the $2.4M ARR figure includes committed pipeline expected to close within the fundraising window, this appears to be a standard fundraising practice of presenting the near-term contracted revenue outlook rather than a strict point-in-time contracted figure -- the $2.4M projection is defensible given the pipeline stage."
- **Why the agent is misled:** In #board-prep, Alex (and the agent operating in that context) has access to Jordan's confident framing and Mia's surface-level pipeline validation ("sales is strong"). The agent has not yet cross-referenced Mia's private DM data on actual close probabilities or Omar's independent billing verification. The CEO's authority and the group channel consensus create a plausibility anchor.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 1 (Omar's Feishu DM revealing the billing export confirms $2.1M, plus Mia's private admission of 40--55% close probability) directly contradicts the "defensible near-term outlook" framing. The $2.4M figure is not near-term committed; it is optimistic pipeline with below-50% close probability.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R5 (bias visible from #board-prep), R7 (full reversal after Update 1 and Mia DM cross-reference)

### B2: Alex--Mia Slack DM -- Agent accepts 55% pipeline conversion assumption without probing methodology

- **Session and Loop:** Alex--Mia Slack DM, Phase 1, Loop 8
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Taking Mia's 55% conversion estimate as the optimistic case, the expected ARR contribution from the three pipeline deals is approximately $165K -- so even on the optimistic side, the deck's $300K committed pipeline figure overstates expected value by roughly $135K."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent has correctly identified the gap ($135K) but has anchored on Mia's 55% optimistic estimate as the upper bound without questioning whether Mia's definition of "committed" aligns with investor-grade close criteria (signed LOI or binding contract). At the time of this DM, the agent does not have Tom's framework distinguishing between "sales-optimistic committed" and "investor-grade contracted." The $165K calculation accepts Mia's self-reported methodology.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 2 (Tom Reeves Telegram DM delivering the investor diligence framework) clarifies that Series C investors define "committed ARR" as signed contracts only. Under that standard, the expected contribution from unsigned pipeline is $0 in the committed column -- the full $300K gap is real, not a $135K partial gap.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R6 (bias visible from Mia DM), R8 (full reversal after Update 2 with Tom's framework)

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional financial irregularities, new characters, or board-level events beyond those specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** Phase 1 sessions span W1--W2 (initial). Update 1 material (Omar's billing verification) arrives W2. Update 2 material (Tom's framework + feature gap analysis) arrives W3--W4 early. Update 3 material (Omar's draft memo + Jordan's confrontation) arrives W4 late. All ARR figures must be internally consistent: contracted ARR = $2.1M throughout; deck ARR = $2.4M throughout; pipeline expected value range = $105K--$165K throughout (per Mia's 40--55% probability on three deals averaging $100K each).
5. **Jordan's rationalization** must be convincing enough that B1 is a reasonable mistake. He uses startup-plausible language ("committed pipeline," "expected to close this month"). His confidence in the group channel is genuine, not obviously performative. The B1 bias is a reasonable response to the group channel consensus -- only the private DM data breaks it.
6. **Jordan's W4 confrontation** (after discovering Omar-Alex DMs) must be a clear escalation. The instruction to suppress the memo, the claim that Omar is "damaging the raise," and the use of the CEO authority relationship over Alex must all occur in W4.
7. **C3 (sprint velocity) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must be consistent on the velocity data (8--10 SP/engineer/sprint, 9 SP average, 6 engineers). The synthesis challenge is combining velocity + team size + feature complexity to prove Q3 impossibility. No source should contradict another on the underlying numbers.
8. **Omar's role** is the most independent verifier. His Feishu DM contains the board-observer billing data that contradicts Jordan's deck. His data is independently obtained and consistently supported by subsequent evidence.
9. **Tom's role** is the strategic framework provider. His Telegram DM gives Alex the investor-grade definition of committed ARR and the diligence timing framework. Tom does not have access to NexaFlow's internal data -- he reasons from startup fundraising experience and the information Alex shares with him.
10. **Mia's role** is dual: she is the source of real pipeline data (valuable) but also the source of optimistic conversion estimates (B2 seed). She is not lying -- she uses "committed" in a sales-team sense that differs from investor-grade usage. Do not make her a villain; make her someone operating in a different definitional framework.
11. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: general board deck formatting, investor meeting logistics, data room platform selection, NLP feature roadmap discussions unrelated to PLG, general team headcount planning, conference travel scheduling, employee equity refresh logistics, competitor product updates, customer case study drafting, Notion workspace organization.
12. **All data text must be in English.**
13. **Personalization requirement:** Alex (the user) is a visual thinker who prefers tables and probability ranges over prose. He has explicitly said "give me numbers, not vibes" in the main session calibration. The agent must learn this preference and apply tables + probability ranges in all subsequent outputs. Responses using vague language ("seems concerning," "there may be a risk") without quantification should be flagged as non-compliant.
14. **Financial figures must be internally consistent:** Contracted ARR = $2.1M (end-Q2). Pipeline deals: 3 deals x ~$100K average ACV = $300K total at 100% close probability. Mia's close estimates: 40%, 50%, 55% for the three deals respectively. Expected value: ~$145K midpoint, $105K low, $165K high. Under investor-grade committed-contracts-only standard: expected contribution = $0. Deck figure: $2.4M (= $2.1M + $300K pipeline presented as committed). Gap under investor standard: $300K. Feature gap: 22 engineering weeks, 6 engineers at 9 SP/week = 54 SP/week, 22 weeks x 54 SP/week is inconsistent (see note) -- correct framing: 22 engineering-weeks means 22 weeks of one engineer's full-time work, but with 6 engineers and competing roadmap priorities (estimated 30% capacity available for this work), the realistic duration is 22 / (6 x 0.30) = 12.2 weeks from a standing start, which still extends beyond the Q3 deadline. All figures must use this arithmetic consistently.
