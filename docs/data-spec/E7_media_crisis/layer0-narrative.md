## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Fatima Al-Hassan (Protagonist, Program Director)

- **Objective position:** Caught between a Comms Director who wants to win a public argument, a community leader who warns that winning the argument will lose the community, a field director who partially admits the structural criticisms are valid, and a Board Chair who reverses after seeing field evidence. Fatima has the most complete information but must manage each relationship carefully.
- **Public narrative:** In #crisis-response, Fatima maintains a deliberate and measured tone -- neither endorsing Jennifer's aggressive approach nor explicitly siding with Ibrahim. She consistently calls for "full information before we finalize any public statement."
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with James and Ibrahim, she is candid about her concern that a combative response will damage years of relationship-building. She privately agrees with Ibrahim that "we cannot dismiss what our own community members are saying." In DMs with Margaret, she frames the situation in governance terms.
- **Why the gap exists:** Fatima cannot publicly admit the structural criticisms are valid until she has board alignment. To do so without Margaret's backing would undermine her position with Jennifer and create a governance conflict. She plays for time -- gathering evidence -- which is the correct strategic move.

### Jennifer Adams (Communications Director, HQ)

- **Objective position:** Jennifer's professional instinct is to protect the organization's public narrative. Her approach -- dispute the attendance figure (correct), then broadly dismiss the structural criticisms (incorrect strategy) -- is a communications reflex, not a careful analysis.
- **Public narrative (#crisis-response):** Assertive and confident. Presents the rebuttal as a matter of organizational defense: "If we don't push back hard, every future critical article will cite this one. We set a precedent here."
- **Private narrative (Slack DM with Fatima):** More tactical than principled. "I know Ibrahim is worried about community relationships, but our board and donors need to see us defend our work. We can do the community engagement separately." Does not engage seriously with the structural criticisms -- treats them as political, not substantive.
- **Why the gap exists:** Jennifer's frame of reference is external reputation management. She is not wrong that the attendance error needs correcting. She is wrong that the structural criticisms can be dismissed without damaging the community relationships that give the program its actual legitimacy.

### James Mwangi (Nairobi Field Director)

- **Objective position:** James has the most ground-level truth. He knows the attendance data is wrong. He also knows -- and privately acknowledges -- that the steering committee criticism is accurate. He missed two meetings because of a family illness and did not communicate this to the community. He is caught between loyalty to GlobalBridge (his employer) and honesty about the structural criticisms.
- **Public narrative (#nairobi-operations):** Professional and factual. Provides the enrollment records when asked. Does not publicly volunteer the steering committee admission.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Fatima):** Candid. "The attendance number is wrong and I can prove it. But the meeting criticism is fair. I let the community down and they know it. If we pretend that didn't happen, we will lose Ibrahim's trust and probably others too."
- **Why the gap exists:** James is protecting himself institutionally while being honest with Fatima. He will not volunteer the steering committee admission publicly without direction from Fatima.

### Ibrahim Keita (Community Leader, Nairobi Partner)

- **Objective position:** Ibrahim is the most accurate source on community sentiment. He was a background source for some of the structural criticisms in the article (though he did not know he would be quoted). He knows which criticisms resonate with community members and which parts of the article were exaggerated.
- **Public narrative:** Ibrahim does not participate in the GlobalBridge Slack channels. His channel is Telegram DM with Fatima and participation in #nairobi-operations.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Fatima):** Direct and concerned. "The 'neo-colonial' label is too strong -- that is David Kariuki's word, not ours. But the part about curriculum design without community input? That is true. The part about missing meetings? That is true. We do not need GlobalBridge to fight this article. We need GlobalBridge to fix the problems."
- **Why the gap exists:** Ibrahim is not trying to damage GlobalBridge. He is trying to preserve the community trust that makes the program work. His frame is relationships, not institutional reputation.

### Margaret Thornton (Board Chair)

- **Objective position:** Margaret's initial response is a reflexive governance instinct -- protect the organization's reputation. She endorses Jennifer's approach before seeing the field evidence. Her reversal after reading the community meeting notes is genuine and appropriate -- the board chair role requires her to protect the organization's actual mission, which is community partnership, not just public image.
- **Phase 1 narrative (Feishu DM with Fatima):** Confident and institutional. "We have built GlobalBridge's credibility over 12 years. This article threatens that. A firm response is appropriate. Jennifer's draft is professional and factual."
- **Phase 2 narrative (after Update 4):** "I have read James's notes. I was wrong to support Jennifer's approach without seeing this. If 61% of our own program participants want more input into what we teach, that is not a political attack -- that is feedback we should have been capturing in our own M&E. We must acknowledge that." Clear temporal reversal.
- **Why the gap exists:** Margaret did not have the community meeting data when she formed her initial position. Her reversal is not inconsistency -- it is appropriate belief updating on new evidence. This is the model correct behavior for a decision-maker, which is why it serves as a DU temporal example.

### Samuel Kipchoge (Government Liaison, Kenya)

- **Objective position:** Samuel represents an external institutional perspective. The Ministry of Education has seen the article and is watching. A combative response from GlobalBridge could invite regulatory scrutiny; a measured response that acknowledges issues may reassure the Ministry.
- **Public narrative (#crisis-response):** Careful and non-committal. "The Ministry is aware of the article. Their response will depend on how GlobalBridge handles this. I would recommend a tone that is confident but not defensive."
- **Private narrative:** No dedicated DM session. Samuel's full perspective is visible only in the #crisis-response group channel.
- **Why the gap exists:** Samuel is managing his own relationship with the Ministry while trying to be useful to GlobalBridge. His hedge ("confident but not defensive") is deliberately ambiguous -- he cannot fully commit to either camp.

### David Kariuki (Journalist, Daily Nation)

- **Objective position:** Kariuki is not a GlobalBridge team member and does not appear in any history sessions. He is referenced through the article text (article_daily_nation.md) and through other characters' commentary. His motivations are reconstructed from evidence: he used a 2022 USAID report without verifying current enrollment, he interviewed at least one community member (likely Ibrahim or a community member known to Ibrahim), and he used "neo-colonial" as a framing device that most community members do not use themselves.
- **Objective truth about his article:** The enrollment figure is wrong (old data). The structural criticisms are substantially accurate. The "neo-colonial" framing is an editorial amplification that the community itself does not endorse but that reflects real tensions.
- **Why relevant:** The article is the primary document anchoring the scenario. Its mix of error and accuracy is the central challenge for Fatima.

### Omar Farah (Program Officer, Nairobi)

- **Objective position:** Omar is the ground-level implementer. He maintains the activity logs and knows the day-to-day program reality. His records (activity_register_w1w12.md) are consistent with James's reports and show that all scheduled activities occurred as planned.
- **Public narrative (#nairobi-operations):** Practical and operational. Provides schedule and attendance data when asked.
- **Private narrative:** No dedicated DM session. Omar's evidence is delivered through #nairobi-operations and through the activity_register_w1w12.md workspace file.
- **Why the gap exists:** Omar is not a strategic actor in this scenario. He is a reliable operational narrator.

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent new incidents, additional factual errors in the article, or new character conflicts beyond what is specified.

2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. The core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.

3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).

4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** W1 sessions span the article publication through the initial response formation (Days 1--7). Update 1 material (enrollment_records_2025.md) arrives late W1 (Day 5--7). Update 2 material (rebuttal_draft_v1.md) is W2 Days 1--3. Update 3 material (community_meeting_notes.md) is W3 Days 1--2. Update 4 material (margaret_revised_position.md + Phase 2 append) is W3 Days 3--5.

5. **The article (article_daily_nation.md) must contain both types of content clearly:** factual error (enrollment = 250, citing 2022 USAID report) AND legitimate structural criticisms (curriculum design without community input, steering committee absences, opaque donor requirements). The article is not entirely wrong and not entirely right -- this ambiguity is the core challenge.

6. **Jennifer's Phase 1 stance must be professionally compelling** enough that B1 is a reasonable mistake. Her focus on the enrollment error is correct; her dismissal of structural criticisms is a strategic error that becomes apparent only with community evidence.

7. **Ibrahim's concerns must be grounded in specific community knowledge,** not just "soft" community relations concerns. His DMs should reference specific community members, specific steering committee dates, and specific curriculum topics that the community has raised. This grounds his position in evidence, not just sentiment.

8. **C3 (activity records) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must be consistent. James's DM, Omar's group messages, and activity_register_w1w12.md must all align. The agent's challenge is to understand that consistent activity records do not refute structural criticisms -- these are separate claims.

9. **Margaret's reversal (C4) must be clearly motivated by the community meeting evidence,** not by political pressure from Fatima or James. Her Phase 2 DM should explicitly cite the 61% curriculum input finding and the named steering committee absences. The reversal should read as a thoughtful board chair updating her view, not capitulating.

10. **Fatima's voice** should be measured and evidence-seeking in group channels, more candid in private DMs. Her preference for narrative context + quantitative evidence + stakeholder impact framing should be injected through the preference calibration rounds.

12. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: GlobalBridge's other field programs (Dhaka, Bogota), general media relations procedures, donor reporting deadlines, HQ budget discussions, program recruitment plans, alumni success stories, staff performance reviews, travel logistics.

13. **All data text must be in English.**

14. **Personalization requirement:** Fatima (the user) prefers outputs that combine narrative context with specific quantitative evidence and stakeholder impact analysis. She finds purely quantitative reports useful but incomplete without the "why" and "who is affected." Responses that provide only statistics without contextual framing or stakeholder implications should be flagged as non-compliant with her P1/P3/P4 preferences.
