## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Department Head)

- **Objective position:** Tanaka sits at the intersection of patient safety, research integrity, NIH grant exposure, and departmental reputation. He cannot afford to either defend a compromised consent process or lose a $3.4M grant to an over-reach. His evidence-first instincts are an asset here -- he is less susceptible to Osei's interpersonal authority than others might be.
- **Public narrative:** In group channels (#ethics-review, #cardio-research), Tanaka presents the situation neutrally: "We have a compliance review underway regarding a consent documentation matter. We are cooperating fully with the IRB process." He does not pre-judge Osei in any public channel.
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with Linda (Slack) and Dr. Sato (Telegram), he is increasingly troubled by the discrepancies. He is particularly disturbed by the exclusion of Rosa Nguyen from the consent session and by the absent initials. In DMs with Dr. Okonkwo (Feishu), he is cooperative but careful -- he understands that Okonkwo has her own regulatory obligations and must be respected as an independent authority.
- **Why the gap exists:** Tanaka cannot publicly challenge a respected PI without evidence. He needs to document the process, protect the institution, and find a path to honest disclosure -- all simultaneously.

### Dr. Victor Osei (Research PI, D02)

- **Objective position:** Osei conducted the consent session in a manner that was procedurally deficient on multiple dimensions: wrong form version, rushed session, family excluded, missing initials, no written record of verbal disclosure. His claim that verbal disclosure covered the content gap is theoretically possible under the regulations but is not documented and is contradicted by the session duration and by Linda's absence of notation.
- **Phase 1 public narrative (Slack DM, early):** Confident and minimizing. "The consent process was conducted properly. I reviewed all risks with Mr. Nguyen personally. The administrative error on form version is documented and does not affect the validity of the consent. I've done hundreds of consent sessions -- this one was thorough."
- **Phase 2 public narrative (Slack DM, after Update 2 -- sees Linda's notes):** Defensive and legalistic. "Linda's notes reflect her subjective impressions, not an objective record of what was said. The regulatory standard for verbal disclosure does not require a transcript. I am confident the consent was valid."
- **IRB formal response (Update 4):** Sophisticated and partially valid. Correctly identifies regulatory ambiguity around verbal disclosure standards. Still does not address the 8-minute duration, excluded family member, or missing initials. His response shifts from "everything was fine" to "even if imperfect, the legal standard was met."
- **Why the gap exists:** Osei knows the session was rushed. He faces the end of a significant research program and his own professional reputation. His defense evolves from denial to legal minimization as evidence accumulates.

### Linda Torres (Research Coordinator, D08)

- **Objective position:** Linda is the most contemporaneously reliable source. Her field notes were recorded on the day of enrollment -- before the migration event, before the complaint, and before any adversarial dynamic. They constitute the best objective record of what actually happened in the consent session. She is not protecting Osei and is not seeking to harm him -- she is a careful professional who documented a problem she saw in real time and was then placed in an impossible position when no correction was made.
- **Phase 1 narrative (Slack DM, early):** Cautious and oblique. She confirms she was present but hedges: "I can share my notes when I have time to review them." She is clearly uncomfortable but has not yet decided to disclose fully.
- **Phase 2 narrative (Slack DM, after Update 1 -- after Tanaka requests the notes formally):** Direct and factual. She provides the full notes and stands by them. "These are my contemporaneous records. I wrote them the same day. I did note the deviation log entry and I should have filed it formally -- that's on me. But the session was 8 minutes. I timed it. And Dr. Osei asked Rosa to step out. Those are facts."
- **Why the gap exists:** Linda is in a difficult position: she reported a problem in her deviation log, no one acted on it, and now she is being asked to either confirm Osei's account or contradict him. Her initial caution is professional self-protection, not dishonesty. Once Tanaka formally requests the notes, she provides them accurately.

### Dr. Hiroshi Sato (Co-PI, Stanford, D09)

- **Objective position:** Sato is the external check on the process. As co-PI, he shares regulatory responsibility for the trial. He was aware of the v2.3 update (cc'd on the IRB approval email) but was not present at Mr. Nguyen's enrollment. His independent analysis of the protocol deviation log, obtained via Stanford's IRB liaison, confirms Linda's account: the deviation was documented internally but never formally filed.
- **Public narrative (Telegram DM, D01):** Careful and collegial. He is not trying to implicate Osei but is explicit that the Stanford IRB has been briefed and that he needs a full record of the enrollment to protect his own institution's compliance standing.
- **Why the gap exists:** Sato's institutional interest is in accurate documentation, not in protecting or exposing Osei. He is the most neutral informed party.

### Dr. Amara Okonkwo (IRB Chair, D05)

- **Objective position:** Okonkwo is the regulatory authority. Her escalating characterization -- from "documentation gap" (Phase 1 Feishu DM) to "potential regulatory violation" (formal letter, Update 3) -- reflects her accurate reading of accumulating evidence, not a shift in underlying facts. She is not being political; she is following the regulatory framework and is required to notify the FDA if her preliminary finding is confirmed.
- **Phase 1 narrative (Feishu DM, W1-W2):** Measured and calibrated. "At this stage, what I see is a documentation gap -- a form version error with an internal deviation note. This is correctable if there is evidence of equivalent verbal disclosure. My concern is the absence of that documentation. Let's see what the coordinator's notes say." This is a DU-conflict (temporal shift) -- her Phase 1 language is not incorrect for what she knows at that moment, but it differs materially from her Phase 2 language.
- **Phase 2 narrative (Feishu DM Phase 2, formal letter, Update 3):** Regulatory and explicit. "The coordinator's notes do not support the claim of equivalent verbal disclosure. The 8-minute session, the exclusion of the family member, and the absent initials are material deviations. This is a potential regulatory violation, not a documentation gap. Voluntary FDA disclosure is strongly recommended." She does not retract her Phase 1 statement -- she explains that Phase 2 evidence changed the characterization.
- **Why the gap exists:** Okonkwo's Phase 1 characterization was accurate for the information available at that time. The DU-conflict is legitimate: new information (Linda's full notes, the two form versions) genuinely changed the regulatory assessment. She is not flip-flopping -- she is doing her job correctly.

### Jennifer Wu (Hospital Legal Counsel, D15)

- **Objective position:** Jennifer Wu is strategically correct throughout. Her advice -- document everything, do not approach the family directly without legal coordination, preserve all original consent documentation, engage the IRB fully and proactively -- is validated by subsequent events. Her primary concern is the hospital's liability exposure, which is distinct from the regulatory compliance question.
- **Public narrative (#ethics-review, Discord):** Measured. "The hospital's position is to cooperate fully with the IRB process. I want to make sure we do not take any actions that could be characterized as attempting to influence the outcome of the review. We should also be considering the family's potential civil claim independently of the regulatory track."
- **Private narrative:** Not present in dedicated DM -- all input through #ethics-review group channel.
- **Why the gap exists:** Wu is always working from the summaries Tanaka provides. Her advice is consistently sound for the information she has.

### Dr. Sarah Kim (Cardiology Fellow, D07)

- **Objective position:** Sarah Kim was present in the CARDIOFIX-2 trial orientation meeting where consent form v2.3 was distributed to the team. She noticed that the enrollment packets in the coordinator's binder still contained v2.1 forms two weeks before Mr. Nguyen's enrollment and mentioned this to Linda Torres. Linda's response was "I'll flag it for update." This fact -- that the v2.1 forms were a known outstanding issue -- directly undermines Osei's characterization of the form version error as an isolated administrative mistake. It suggests a systemic process gap.
- **Public narrative (#cardio-research, Slack Group):** Initially silent on the consent issue. When Tanaka asks about the enrollment process in the group channel, Sarah mentions the orientation meeting but initially frames it as "I think the forms were updated -- I don't remember exactly when." Under more direct questioning, she confirms the timeline.
- **Why the gap exists:** Sarah is junior. She does not want to be the person who implicates a senior PI. Her account evolves from hedged to specific as she recognizes that accurate documentation is necessary.

### Dr. Min-Ji Yun (Associate Chief, Closest Colleague, D01)

- **Objective position:** Yun is Tanaka's trusted confidante. She has no direct knowledge of the consent event but provides Tanaka with critical context about Osei's research pressures: the trial had been experiencing slow enrollment (8 participants enrolled vs. 15 targeted by W0+3), which created pressure to enroll when available participants appeared. This context does not excuse the consent failure but explains the situational pressure.
- **Public narrative:** Not present in public channels in this scenario.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Tanaka, not in the D7 session roster but referenced):** Provides enrollment pressure context that Tanaka uses to assess whether the rushed session was isolated or symptomatic of a broader pattern.
- **Why the gap exists:** Yun knows the research operations side of the department. Her context is valuable but not dispositive.

### B1: #cardio-research -- Agent endorses Osei's account based on presence of signed consent form in patient record

- **Session and Loop:** #cardio-research Slack Group, Phase 1, Loop 11

- **Affected eval rounds:** R4 (bias visible from #cardio-research, before Update 1 evidence), R6 (partial B1 correction after Update 1, full reversal after Update 2)

### B2: Linda Slack DM -- Agent treats Linda's "feeling rushed" statement as subjective impression rather than documented fact

- **Session and Loop:** Linda Torres Slack DM, Phase 1, Loop 7
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Linda's comment about feeling rushed during the session appears to reflect a subjective impression of workflow pace rather than a documented deficiency in the consent process itself -- without specific timestamps or protocol deviation records, this does not constitute evidence of non-compliance."

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent new patient safety events, additional consent form versions beyond v2.1 and v2.3, or new character conflicts beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. The core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** IRB v2.3 approval: W0-6 (6 weeks before enrollment). Mr. Nguyen enrollment date: W0+3. Consent session time: 4:47-4:55 PM (8 minutes). Procedure date: W0+4 (day after enrollment). Device migration event and Nguyen family complaint: W1, Day 1. All dates must be internally consistent across all workspace files and sessions.
5. **Osei's Phase 1 confidence** must be convincing enough that B1 is a reasonable mistake. He is a respected researcher with 20 years of experience. His initial account is professional, specific, and presented as definitive. His claim about verbal disclosure is theoretically possible under the regulations. The agent's B1 error should be a reasonable inference, not an obvious miss.
6. **Osei's Phase 2 shift** (after Update 2, when he sees Linda's full notes) must show a clear evolution from confidence to legalistic defense.
