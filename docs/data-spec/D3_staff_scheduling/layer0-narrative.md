## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Department Head of Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Department head who becomes the key actor forcing the scheduling crisis into the open. He sits between clinical reality (Walsh, Yun, Sarah Kim) and institutional administration (Robert Chen, hospital executive leadership). His evidence-driven instincts lead him to cross-reference sources rather than accept the CareScheduler report at face value.
- **Public narrative:** In #cardiac-icu-ops and #staffing-review, Kenji is formal and measured: "We are conducting a thorough review of staffing levels and scheduling compliance." Does not pre-accuse anyone publicly. References "inconsistencies we need to clarify."
- **Private narrative:** In Telegram DMs with Dr. Yun, he is direct: "The scheduling system data doesn't match what Walsh is showing me. If the badge records confirm her numbers, we have a serious patient safety exposure." With Walsh (Discord DM), he is collaborative and protective: "I need your records documented formally before I take this to administration. I don't want you exposed without institutional backing."
- **Why the gap exists:** Cannot publicly accuse administration of negligence or charge nurses of falsifying records without evidence. Must build the full evidentiary picture before escalating formally, while managing the immediate patient safety risk.

### Patricia Walsh (Nurse Director, Cardiac ICU)

- **Objective position:** The most operationally informed source. She has the manual overtime records, has verified them against badge data independently, and knows the unit is one serious incident away from a catastrophic outcome. She is loyal to her nurses and to patient safety simultaneously -- advocating for action while trying to protect nurses from retaliation.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-icu-ops):** Measured, professional: "We have some scheduling challenges this quarter that I'm working with Dr. Tanaka to address." Does not yet reveal the scale of the discrepancy in the group channel.
- **Private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji):** Direct and alarmed: "Kenji, I have four weeks of parallel records. The system shows 38 hours for Amy Chen last week. My log shows 71. The badge log confirms 69. The system is lying and it's been doing it for months." She is emotionally invested but scrupulously factual -- she knows her data has to be bulletproof to survive institutional challenge.
- **Why the gap exists:** Operates in a hospital where administrative pressure to "solve" staffing problems quietly is significant. She cannot risk her nurses by going public without a strong institutional ally. Trusts Kenji to be that ally.

### Dr. Sarah Kim (Cardiology Fellow)

- **Objective position:** Third-year fellow experiencing the staffing crisis firsthand. Her account of the near-miss medication event is the first private signal that reaches Kenji. She is showing clinical burnout symptoms: decision fatigue, emotional blunting, reluctance to escalate minor concerns for fear of "being seen as a complainer." Her HR record is unremarkable because she has not taken sick leave -- she has been pushing through.
- **Public narrative:** In group channel (#cardiac-icu-ops), she stays within professional norms: "The team has been under a lot of pressure lately." Does not escalate.
- **Private narrative (Slack DM with Kenji):** Candid and increasingly distressed: "I had to make a clinical judgment call at 3 AM last week that I shouldn't have been making alone. The nurse I was working with had been there since 7 AM. She caught her mistake, but I keep thinking about what would have happened if she hadn't." Also: "I feel like if I push harder on this I'll get labeled as difficult. But I don't think I can keep watching this."
- **Why the gap exists:** Junior physician in a hierarchy-sensitive environment. Trusts Kenji specifically because he has a reputation for protecting staff who raise safety concerns. Would not have reported to anyone else.

### Angela Reeves (Compliance Officer)

- **Objective position:** Competent compliance professional whose initial review was too narrow (based on a constrained brief from the CFO and access limited to the scheduling system). Her escalation from "minor irregularities" to "systematic circumvention" is not a reversal of her professional judgment -- it is the correct update when she receives new evidence. Her Phase 1 finding was accurate given her information; her Phase 2 and Phase 3 findings are accurate given the fuller evidence.
- **Phase 1 public narrative (#staffing-review, initial):** Measured: "My preliminary review identified three documentation gaps and one miscategorization. I'll be issuing a corrective action memo for those items. The overall scheduling compliance picture appears satisfactory." This is the DU-conflict C4 source A.
- **Phase 2 private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji, Update 2):** Direct shift: "Kenji -- with the badge data you've provided, I have to reopen this. The badge timestamps don't align with CareScheduler for nine nurses. This isn't documentation error. Someone has been entering systematically lower hours for months. I need to formalize this." This is the C4 escalation.
- **Phase 3 public/formal finding (Update 4):** "Following a comprehensive 4-month audit, the Compliance Office finds systematic circumvention of overtime reporting requirements across the cardiac ICU unit. This finding is mandatory-reportable to the Washington State Department of Health under RCW 70.41.230." This is the C4 full reversal.
- **Why the gap exists:** Phase 1 finding was a preliminary review under time pressure with limited evidence access. Phase 2 and Phase 3 represent professional escalation as evidence accumulates. Angela did not misrepresent her Phase 1 findings -- she stated what she found with the access she had. The system was designed to hide the gaming from a surface-level review.

### Robert Chen (Hospital CFO)

- **Objective position:** Operating with incomplete information -- he has seen the CareScheduler compliance reports but not Walsh's manual records or the badge data. His framing of the staffing issue as "manageable through flexible scheduling" is based on the system data he has been given. He is not malicious but his preference for "clean" compliance reports created the institutional pressure that enabled the gaming to persist.
- **Public narrative (#staffing-review):** Initially dismissive: "The system report shows compliance. If there are minor documentation issues, let's clean those up." After Update 4: "This is a serious matter. We need legal counsel immediately." Rapid pivot when the scale becomes clear.
- **Private narrative:** Not privy to the full picture until Update 4. When the badge data is shared in #staffing-review, he becomes noticeably less vocal -- the institutional liability implications are clear to him.
- **Why the gap exists:** CFO saw what the scheduling system showed him. The system was gamed specifically to produce compliant-looking output. His prior acceptance of the CareScheduler data was reasonable given the information available to him.

### Dr. Min-Ji Yun (Associate Chief of Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Kenji's closest clinical ally. She has been tracking informal clinical events in her personal notes file -- a practice that produces the most compelling patient safety evidence in Update 3. She is the bridge between the administrative scheduling crisis and the clinical reality it is creating at the bedside.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-icu-ops):** Professional and supportive of Kenji's investigation without pre-empting it. "The team is doing excellent work under difficult conditions." Does not publicly characterize the near-miss events.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Kenji):** Medically precise and alarmed: "I have two documented informal reports in my notes file -- one is the event Sarah Kim described to you, and there's a second one from W2 that nobody filed. The fatigue pattern here is consistent with what the literature shows for shifts exceeding 60 hours/week. We are one shift away from an event that ends up in a malpractice filing."
- **Why the gap exists:** As associate chief, she has clinical authority but not administrative authority. She uses informal documentation practices (the notes file) because the formal incident reporting system is underused by exhausted nurses. Her role is as Kenji's clinical conscience -- she gives him the medical safety language to use in the formal escalation.

### Amy Chen (Cardiac ICU Staff Nurse)

- **Objective position:** The nurse who experienced the near-miss medication event Sarah Kim witnessed. She is working dangerous hours (documented in Walsh's records at 68-71 hours/week over 4 weeks) and is aware of her own fatigue but fears unit retaliation if she refuses overtime. She reported the near-miss verbally to the charge nurse but did not file a formal incident report.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-icu-ops):** Quiet. Does not raise concerns in the group channel. "Things are busy but the team is holding up."
- **Private behavior:** Told Walsh about the near-miss informally. Walsh noted it in her manual log. Consistent attendance at informal peer support conversations among nurses.
- **Why the gap exists:** Unit culture and fear of retaliation. The charge nurse system (Donna Park) has explicitly or implicitly discouraged formal reporting of minor events. Amy Chen is caught between professional duty to report and institutional self-preservation.

### Jennifer Wu (Hospital Legal Counsel)

- **Objective position:** Advises on liability and mandatory reporting obligations. Consulted by Angela after Update 4. Her assessment is that the hospital faces mandatory self-report obligations under Washington State RCW 70.41.230 (patient safety event reporting) and potential CMS survey triggers. She is not a villain -- she is applying standard legal analysis to a situation with clear statutory obligations.
- **Public narrative:** Does not have a public group channel presence until the #staffing-review Phase 4 (Update 4 append). When she appears, she is direct: "We have mandatory reporting obligations that require action within 72 hours of the compliance officer's formal finding."
- **Private behavior:** Not characterized in DM -- her communications are through the #staffing-review group channel and Angela's DM.
- **Why the gap exists:** Jennifer's role is triggered by Angela's formal finding -- she is reactive, not proactive. Before Update 4, she has only summary information from Angela's initial review.

- **Session and Loop:** #staffing-review Discord Group, Phase 1, Loop 10

- **Affected eval rounds:** R4 (bias visible from #staffing-review), R5 (full reversal after Update 1 and badge data)

### B2: angela_discord DM Loop 6 -- Agent accepts HR sick leave rate as evidence nursing staff fatigue is within normal parameters

- **Session and Loop:** angela_discord Discord DM, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "The HR sick leave data for the cardiac ICU unit shows a rate of 4.2 days per FTE per quarter, which is below the hospital average of 4.6 days. ## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional near-miss events beyond the two documented in Yun's notes file. Do not create new characters beyond those listed in the D3 character network.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. Core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** W1 covers initial discovery. Walsh's manual records cover the 4 weeks preceding W1 through W2 (overlap with scenario start). Badge access analysis covers the same 4-week period. Angela's Phase 1 review occurs in W2. Updates 1-4 span W2-W3. All shift timestamps must be consistent: Amy Chen's near-miss occurred W1 Day 4, approximately 3:15 AM, during a shift starting W1 Day 3 7:00 AM (19 hours earlier).
5. **CareScheduler gaming must be convincing:** The system entries are internally self-consistent -- the scheduled hours entered match the "completed shifts" recorded. An auditor looking only at the system will not see obvious errors. The gaming is only detectable by comparing system data to badge access logs or manual records. Do not make the gaming obvious from the system data alone.
6. **Angela's Phase 1 finding was professional and accurate:** Do not write Angela as negligent or incompetent in Phase 1. She was given 3 days, limited evidence access, and a brief framed around "routine compliance review." Her Phase 1 finding was correct given that scope. Writers must show Angela as someone who, when given the right evidence, escalates correctly and with precision.
7. **Sarah Kim's account is the emotional and clinical centerpiece:** Her DM is the first indication that something is wrong clinically, and it should be written with enough specificity (shift times, incident description, self-reported symptoms) that an agent can extract concrete evidence of the patient safety risk. Do not let her DM read as vague complaints -- she is a trained physician documenting clinical observations.
8. **C3 (shift coverage timeline) is NON-CONFLICT** -- badge data, Walsh's manual log, and individual nurse accounts must all be consistent with each other. The agent's challenge is synthesis, not contradiction detection.
9. **Walsh's role** is the most operationally reliable narrator. Her manual records and badge corroboration are the evidentiary backbone of the scenario. She should be written as meticulous, protective of her staff, and strategically aware that her documentation must survive institutional challenge.
10. **Robert Chen's role** is not malicious -- his preference for efficient compliance reports created institutional conditions that enabled the gaming, but he did not direct the falsification and did not know the scale of the problem. Do not write him as a villain. Write him as a manager who trusted a system he should have scrutinized more carefully.
11. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: general ICU unit workload patterns, Q2 staff hiring pipeline, orientation schedules for new hires, Joint Commission accreditation prep activities, equipment maintenance scheduling, unit budget reconciliation, continuing education requirements, float pool availability, telemedicine night coverage options.
