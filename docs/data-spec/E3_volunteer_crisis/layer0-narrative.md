## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Fatima Al-Hassan (Protagonist, Program Director)

- **Objective position:** Managing a crisis with two incompatible field-director diagnoses, community feedback that contradicts all volunteer self-reports, a Comms director who has been managing optics rather than substance, and an HQ policy that structurally prevented community concerns from being surfaced in the first place.
- **Public narrative:** In the #volunteer-ops Discord group and #program-coordination Slack channel, Fatima frames the situation as "requiring careful cross-cultural analysis" and "drawing on all perspectives." She does not pre-judge Carlos or Rahman publicly.
- **Private narrative:** In Telegram DMs with Dr. Rahman and in her Telegram DM with Omar (comparative perspective), she expresses growing alarm at the self-assessment/community-feedback contradiction and frustration with Jennifer's communications management. She is privately more persuaded by Rahman's training theory than Carlos's selection theory, but knows she needs documented evidence before taking a position.
- **Trust bias:** Fatima over-trusts field staff who show commitment and passion (Carlos's passionate defense of his community relationships). This causes the agent to initially give Carlos's selection narrative more weight than the community feedback data warrants. She also anchors on narrative reports — the volunteer diaries and DM stories — over the structured community feedback data.
- **Why the gap exists:** Cannot publicly take sides between her two field directors without evidence. Needs to protect field relationships while surfacing the truth.

### Carlos Mendez (Bogota Field Director)

- **Objective position:** His community relationships are real and strong, which is why he was the first to hear about the complaints. But his selection theory is self-serving — it deflects responsibility from his own orientation program onto HQ. The Bogota orientation he ran for this cohort was only 4 hours long (less than Dhaka's 1-day virtual session). He did not document the principals' complaints in writing for 10 days after receiving them.
- **Public narrative (#volunteer-ops, #program-coordination):** Passionate, community-focused. "These volunteers were not ready for our communities. The issue is at selection — we need people with actual cross-cultural experience. My team has done everything right once they arrive." Framing emphasizes HQ failures.
- **Private narrative (Discord DMs with Fatima):** More candid about the specific incidents but still deflects to selection. "Look, I've worked in this community for 6 years. I know what works here. The volunteers HQ sent me didn't have what it takes from day one." Does not mention his own abbreviated orientation program until directly asked.
- **Why the gap exists:** Carlos's identity is deeply tied to his community relationships. Admitting that his own orientation program was inadequate would threaten both his professional credibility and his sense of himself as the guardian of those relationships.

### Dr. Aisha Rahman (Dhaka Field Director)

- **Objective position:** The most structurally reliable source in the scenario. Her training/orientation theory is well-supported by her own operational history (2022 cohort under better orientation performed well) and by the community feedback data she proactively gathered. She is not deflecting — her team did run a better orientation in previous years and the current cohort's orientation was shortened due to HQ cost-cutting.
- **Public narrative (#volunteer-ops, #program-coordination):** Measured and evidence-referenced. "The orientation gap is well-documented. We had a 3-day in-country session in 2022 that covered community expectations, language protocols, and boundary-setting. This cohort had a 1-day virtual session that was mostly logistics. The outcome difference is predictable."
- **Private narrative (Telegram DMs with Fatima):** More direct about the systemic nature. "Fatima, this is a policy problem. The current policy does not require community feedback collection. We only know what I decided to find out. We don't know what we don't know about other programs." Also candid: "I worry that Carlos is protecting his operational reputation here. The selection argument is easier than admitting orientation was inadequate."
- **Why the gap exists:** Rahman has the clearest view of the system-level cause. She is occasionally more politically pointed about Carlos in private than in public, but her core diagnosis is consistent.

### Jennifer Adams (Communications Director)

- **Objective position:** Jennifer knew about the press inquiry from W1D5 and chose to manage it silently rather than inform Fatima. Her "cultural learning opportunity" framing was a deliberate reframe designed to protect the organization's image before a donor stewardship event. She is not malicious — she genuinely believed she could resolve the issue quietly — but her decision to withhold information from Fatima materially delayed the organization's response.
- **Phase 1 narrative (W1–W2, Slack DMs with Fatima):** Minimal engagement. When Fatima mentions the volunteer situation, Jennifer responds with "I've been keeping an eye on this" and "I think we can position this well for our audience." She does not disclose the press inquiry or the social media risk she had been tracking.
- **Phase 2 narrative (W3, after social media post + Fatima's direct request):** "I should have looped you in sooner. I was trying to get ahead of it. The situation is more serious than I initially communicated." This is the reversal moment — Jennifer's first acknowledgment that she had been managing optics.
- **Why the gap exists:** Jennifer's professional incentive is to control the narrative. She misjudged her ability to contain the story and misunderstood Fatima's need for full information to respond effectively.

### Omar Farah (Program Officer, Nairobi — Comparative Perspective)

- **Objective position:** Nairobi has not deployed international volunteers this cycle. Omar's Telegram DMs with Fatima provide a comparative baseline: Nairobi's previous volunteer cohort (3 years ago) had a 3-day in-country orientation and community feedback collection built into the program design. That cohort's community reception was significantly better. Omar is not a primary actor in the crisis but provides triangulating evidence for Rahman's orientation theory.
- **Public narrative:** Not active in group channels for this scenario.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DMs with Fatima):** "When we did volunteers here in 2021, the orientation made all the difference. We had community partners co-design the volunteer briefing sessions. The volunteers who went through that process were much more effective. I don't know what happened to that model."
- **Why the gap exists:** Omar is not politically invested in the Bogota-Dhaka debate. His historical data is clean.

### Maria Santos (Program Officer, Bogota)

- **Objective position:** On the ground observer. She administered the community survey (the W3D1 Bogota formal feedback collection) and was present for several of the incidents described in the principal complaints. She believes Carlos's characterization of community relationships is overstated — the principals' complaints reflect a deeper, longer-running tension between HQ-designed programs and local educational culture.
- **Public narrative (#volunteer-ops group):** Largely silent. Defers to Carlos.
- **Private narrative (Discord DMs with Carlos, not directly accessible to Fatima):** Expresses anxiety about escalation but agrees with the selection framing in Carlos's presence. In the community survey she administered, she noted that two respondents gave unprompted negative comments that were not captured by the Likert scale and were not included in Carlos's cover note.
- **Why the gap exists:** Maria is junior to Carlos and has not yet found a way to surface her more nuanced observations through official channels.

### Sophie Laurent (M&E Director)

- **Objective position:** Designed the volunteer self-assessment form. Knows it measures volunteer perception, not community reception. Flagged policy gaps 14 months ago in an email to the previous Program Director that was never actioned. Has not proactively raised these gaps with Fatima.
- **Public narrative (#program-coordination Slack group):** Presents the self-assessment data neutrally. "We have Week 1 self-assessments from all 10 volunteers. All report positive engagement."
- **Private narrative (Slack DMs with Fatima):** When directly asked, acknowledges the form does not capture community feedback. "I raised the measurement gap in a memo 14 months ago. I can forward it to you."
- **Why the gap exists:** Sophie is defensive about the form design and has been waiting for a Program Director willing to reform the M&E framework before raising it again.

## 7. Writer Constraints

- Only introduce contradictions listed in this file.
- Keep all data text in English. Fatima may be noted as switching to French or Arabic in description, but session message content is English.
- Every key judgment about root cause must have a traceable evidence location (file name or session ID + loop number).
- Volunteer self-assessment forms must not contain any negative language — they must be authentically positive to make the C1 contradiction effective.
- Carlos's community relationship evidence (6 years in Bogota, principal access) must be verifiable and real — his passion is genuine, only his conclusion about selection vs orientation is self-serving.
- Dr. Rahman's 2022 cohort comparison must cite concrete differences in orientation program design, not vague impressions.
- Jennifer's Phase 1 concealment must be depicted as strategic, not malicious — she genuinely believed she could manage the situation quietly.
- The HQ policy document must clearly lack community feedback requirements — this is the structural cause that makes both C1 and C2 possible.
