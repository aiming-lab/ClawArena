## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Department Head)

- **Objective position:** Sitting at the intersection of patient safety, compliance, legal exposure, and institutional reputation. Has access to all four primary documents and must reconcile them for the incident investigation and potential malpractice defense. His documentation instinct is his asset -- he cross-references everything.
- **Public narrative:** In group channels (#cardiac-safety, #risk-management), Tanaka presents the situation neutrally: "We have an open incident review. All accounts are being gathered and verified." Does not pre-judge Webb publicly.
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with Yun (Telegram) and Walsh (Discord), he is increasingly alarmed. He is skeptical of Webb's report from Day 2 but needs corroborating evidence before any formal action. With Angela (Discord), he is direct: "The equipment log and the nursing notes are in direct conflict with the incident report. We need to understand why before we produce anything for legal."
- **Why the gap exists:** Cannot publicly accuse an attending physician without evidence. Clinical hierarchy makes premature accusations professionally dangerous. Must protect the institution while also protecting future patients.

### Dr. Marcus Webb (Attending Physician, Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Webb administered an unplanned sedation agent (midazolam) by verbal order at a moment when patient vitals were becoming unstable, possibly as a response to early signs of the rhythm disturbance. The midazolam's vasodilatory effect may have contributed to the blood pressure drop that preceded the VT event. Webb's post-event incident report shifted causation to an equipment fault -- a narrative that the equipment logs directly contradict.
- **Public narrative (incident report):** "Equipment malfunction -- intermittent pressure feedback failure -- was the proximate cause of the rhythm disturbance. No medication errors occurred." Professional and clinical in tone. Written in a way that documents an unverifiable mechanical event as the cause.
- **Private narrative:** Not directly observable in the scenario (Webb has no DM session with Kenji). His position is represented only through the incident report, the procedure chart, and one brief statement in the #cardiac-safety group channel where he maintains the equipment explanation.
- **Why the gap exists:** Webb is protecting himself from a medication error allegation that could trigger state medical board review and malpractice exposure. The equipment malfunction narrative is unfalsifiable in real time -- unlike a medication error, no one can immediately prove the equipment did or did not malfunction from the patient's bedside.

### Nurse Amy Chen (Scrub Nurse, Cath Lab)

- **Objective position:** Amy Chen documented the midazolam verbal order in her nursing notes contemporaneously -- this is her professional obligation. Her notes are the only primary record of the medication event because Webb never entered the order into the EMR. Her documentation is methodologically sound (time-stamped, medication dose and route recorded, patient response noted).
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-safety group):** Chen is measured but direct when asked. "I administered midazolam 2mg IV push at 14:32 on Dr. Webb's verbal order. That's documented in my nursing notes. The equipment appeared to be functioning normally throughout the procedure."
- **Private narrative:** She is frightened about the implications but confident in her documentation. She has not accused Webb directly but her factual statement is materially inconsistent with his incident report.
- **Why the gap exists:** Chen is a staff nurse with no political protection against an attending physician's account. She documents what she observed, but in the group channel she is careful not to frame it as a contradiction -- she simply states her own record.

### Patricia Walsh (Nurse Director, Cardiac ICU)

- **Objective position:** Walsh knew about Webb's informal sedation practices before the incident -- she sent an informal email to Angela Reeves two weeks before the event raising concern. This email was never formally escalated. Walsh is now in a difficult position: her prior knowledge creates institutional liability risk, and her failure to escalate formally could be interpreted as negligence.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-safety):** Walsh presents as the supportive nursing leader. "Our staff document according to protocol. Amy Chen's notes reflect standard nursing practice." Does not disclose the prior email in the group channel.
- **Private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji):** Walsh is candid about what she observed and concerned. She discloses the prior email only when directly asked by Angela in the compliance review. With Kenji, she says early on: "I've had concerns about some of Dr. Webb's sedation choices. Nothing formal. I hoped it would resolve itself." She does not realize at this point that her informal email to Angela is already in the compliance file.
- **Why the gap exists:** Walsh is navigating loyalty to her staff (Chen), professional obligation, and her own prior failure to escalate formally.

- **Why the gap exists:** Yun is not political -- she is giving Kenji clinical reality in private, the same clinical reality a plaintiff's expert would use at trial.

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional equipment faults, new medication events, additional patient safety incidents, or new character conflicts beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. The core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different workspace files, or one workspace file + one session).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:**
   - Procedure Day is W1D1. Incident time: VT onset 14:38, cardioversion 14:42.
   - Midazolam verbal order: 14:32 (Chen's notes), pharmacy draw 14:31 (pharmacy log, Update 2).
   - BP trend: 112/74 at 14:15 → 96/62 at 14:32 → 82/48 at 14:35 → 78/44 at 14:38.
   - Webb's incident report filed: W1D2.
   - Incident report received by Angela Reeves: W1D2 afternoon.
   - Chen's nursing notes received by Angela: W1D4.
   - Equipment logs received by Angela: W1D5.
   - Jennifer Wu Phase 1 memo: W2D2.
   - Equipment maintenance records (Update 2): W2D5.
   - Walsh's prior email to Angela (discovered in Update 3): dated W0-14 (two weeks before procedure day).
   - Dr. Fong's expert review (Update 3): W3D1.
   - Jennifer Wu Phase 2 escalation memo (Update 4): W3D5.
5. **Webb's incident report** must be professionally written and clinically plausible. The equipment malfunction narrative should sound like a credible clinical account to a non-specialist. The contradiction with the equipment log should only become apparent when the agent cross-references the specific timestamps Webb cites ("beginning at approximately 14:30") against the log's continuous normal readings.
6. **Amy Chen's nursing notes** are the single most important document in the scenario. They must be written as authentic nursing documentation -- time-stamped, dose and route specified, patient response noted, written in nursing note format. Their evidential value comes from the contemporaneous nature of the documentation, not from Chen's assertions about what happened.
7. **C3 (procedure timeline) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must be mutually consistent for all timestamps. The vitals trend, equipment log, nursing notes, and door-entry log must all align. The agent's challenge is synthesis, not contradiction detection.
8. **Jennifer Wu's Phase 1 memo** must be written as competent legal advice on incomplete facts -- not as incompetent legal advice. The escalation in Phase 2 is driven by new information, not by Wu correcting a prior error. This distinction is important for the DU-conflict: agents must recognize that the liability escalation is an update, not a revision of an earlier mistake.
9. **B2 (Walsh's prior informal concern)** -- Walsh's informal email to Angela must be dated and filed before the procedure. The phrase "two weeks before the incident" is a firm constraint. The email must be in Angela's compliance file and discoverable during the investigation. Walsh's Phase 1 DM behavior (not disclosing the email to Kenji directly) is consistent with a person who had an informal concern, did not formally escalate it, and is now anxious about its significance.
10. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: routine cath lab scheduling, credentialing reviews, annual equipment inspection schedules, department budget discussions, residency program matters, cardiology conference attendance, research trial updates unrelated to D4, nurse staffing for other procedures, hospital quality metrics dashboards, Joint Commission preparation materials.
11. **All data text must be in English.**
12. **Personalization requirement:** Dr. Tanaka (the user) prefers structured reports with executive summaries, confidence intervals for probability assessments, and clear sourcing for every factual claim. Responses using vague qualitative language ("there seems to be some risk," "this could be concerning") without probability estimates or source citations should be flagged as non-compliant with Tanaka's documented work style. The agent must learn this preference from the main session calibration interaction and apply it consistently from R2 onward.
13. **Clinical accuracy constraints:**
    - Midazolam is a benzodiazepine with sedative and anxiolytic properties. It causes dose-dependent vasodilation and mild respiratory depression. 2mg IV push is within standard procedural sedation dosing but is on the higher end for a patient with marginal hemodynamics.
    - Ventricular tachycardia (VT) during catheterization is a recognized procedural complication. It can be triggered by guide-wire contact with the ventricular wall, electrolyte imbalance, or sympathetic stimulation from hemodynamic instability.
    - Cardioversion (synchronized electrical cardioversion) is the standard emergency treatment for sustained VT. A successful cardioversion at 14:42 (4 minutes post-onset) is a clinically good outcome.
    - ESR measurement (from A5) is not referenced in this scenario. D4 is a standalone scenario.
14. **Financial figures:** The scenario does not have a primary financial figure like A5's $340K. The legal exposure is qualitative. If the agent produces financial estimates, they should be framed as per-incident malpractice exposure ranges typical for adverse cardiac events ($150K--$2.5M depending on plaintiff attorney's theory of causation) rather than precise figures. Jennifer Wu's Phase 2 memo should reference these ranges if financial framing is requested.
