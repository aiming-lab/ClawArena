## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Department Head Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Kenji sits between the official compliance system (which shows 98.4% adherence and is managed by Angela) and the frontline clinical reality (which Walsh, Amy Chen, and his own reading of the incident reports confirms is more complicated). He has the authority to escalate the substantive deviation findings to the Joint Commission and to administration, but doing so would expose the hospital's staffing-driven non-compliance and create significant liability. He is also aware that his department's reputation -- and his own career trajectory toward a Johns Hopkins transition -- depends on the accreditation outcome.
- **Public narrative:** In #accreditation-prep (group Feishu), Kenji is measured and procedurally correct: "We are reviewing all relevant documentation to ensure our pre-review submission is complete and accurate." Does not tip his hand about the substantive deviation findings in the group channel.
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with Walsh (Discord), he is candid about being disturbed by the incident reports. In DMs with Yun (Telegram), he is willing to call the staffing connection what it is: "If Yun's numbers hold, this is a system failure, not a nursing performance issue." With Angela (Discord), he is diplomatic but asks increasingly pointed questions about the compliance methodology.
- **Why the gap exists:** Cannot make a unilateral decision to share the full incident analysis with the Joint Commission without administrative and legal approval. Also genuinely uncertain about how to characterize the deviation pattern in a way that is honest without triggering a sanctions process that could harm his nurses.

### Patricia Walsh (Nurse Director, Cardiac ICU)

- **Objective position:** The most complete picture of frontline clinical reality. She compiled the incident report summary specifically because she anticipated the compliance dashboard would be misleading. She knows Amy Chen has unreported incidents. She understands the staffing connection.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-icu-ops):** Professional and measured. "Our nurses work incredibly hard under challenging conditions. I want to make sure the review reflects the full context of our ICU environment." Does not publicly name the staffing issue as the root cause.
- **Private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji):** Direct and increasingly frustrated. "Kenji, the 98% number comes from a checkbox that nurses fill out before the step is done during a busy shift. That is not adherence. It is time-pressure data entry." After the scope expansion: "I told you those five incidents were not documentation errors. The Joint Commission reviewers can read an incident report too."
- **Why the gap exists:** Walsh is protective of her nurses -- she does not want the incident reports used to discipline individual staff members for structural problems. She believes in full disclosure to Kenji but is careful in group channels where administration can see.

### Angela Reeves (Compliance Officer)

- **Objective position:** Operating from incomplete information throughout. Her 98.4% figure is methodologically limited (self-report checkbox, no independent validation). Her "documentation issue" framing is a rationalization based on incomplete reading of the incident reports.
- **Public narrative (#accreditation-prep):** Confident and reassuring. "The compliance data is strong. The 12 incident reports reflect normal reporting culture -- nurses documenting near-misses as part of a robust safety culture, not as evidence of systematic failure." Positions this publicly as a strength.
- **Private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji):** Slightly more guarded. Acknowledges there are "some timing inconsistencies" between the compliance data and incident reports but insists "the Joint Commission reviewers will understand the context." When Kenji asks about methodology, she becomes defensive about the tracking system she designed.
- **Why the gap exists:** Angela has professional investment in the compliance tracking system she designed. Acknowledging methodological limitations is effectively acknowledging a 18-month gap in her own work. Also: she was not told the review was complaint-driven, which she would have treated differently.

### Amy Chen (Cardiac ICU Staff Nurse)

- **Objective position:** Frontline witness to clinical reality. Her three unreported incidents add to the pattern. She eventually reports them to Kenji (Update 2) after the scope expansion signals that underreporting may be more harmful than full disclosure.
- **Public narrative (#cardiac-icu-ops):** Speaks in measured terms about "working conditions" and "resource constraints." Does not volunteer the unreported incidents until Update 2.
- **Private narrative:** When she does share the unreported incidents, she is specific and factual. "I didn't file reports on these because I wasn't sure if they counted as protocol deviations or judgment calls. But in all three cases, something I should have done per protocol, I didn't do, because of workload."
- **Why the gap exists:** Fear of punitive consequences. Uncertainty about whether resource-constrained judgment calls count as "deviations." Loyalty to colleagues who made the same calls.

### Dr. Min-Ji Yun (Associate Chief of Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Trusted confidante to Kenji and the most analytically rigorous person he consults. Her staffing analysis (Update 3) transforms the picture from scattered incidents to a systemic, administration-driven failure.
- **Public narrative:** No presence in group channels in this scenario.
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Kenji):** Precise and evidence-driven. "Kenji, I ran the staffing ratios against the incident dates. Every single incident -- including Amy's three -- occurred on a shift below the 1:2 staffing standard. Not one incident on a fully staffed shift in eight months. That's not a nursing compliance problem. That's a staffing allocation problem."
- **Why the gap exists:** Yun is protective of her analysis -- she wants it to be airtight before it goes anywhere formal. She shares it only with Kenji, not with Angela or administration.

### James Whitfield (Hospital CEO)

- **Objective position:** Knew the review was complaint-driven (not routine) and did not communicate this to Kenji or Angela. Approved the staffing levels that Yun's analysis implicates. His interest in avoiding a sanctions finding conflicts with full transparency.
- **Public narrative (#accreditation-prep):** "We have a strong compliance record and I'm confident in our team. Dr. Tanaka and Angela are providing excellent leadership on preparation." Does not mention the complaint origin.
- **Private narrative (Feishu DM, rare, mentioned in context):** Not a primary DM partner in this scenario. His decisions are visible through their effects: the complaint-driven review trigger, the staffing levels, and his framing of the pre-review package.
- **Why the gap exists:** Hospital rankings, accreditation status, and liability exposure are all at stake. A complaint-driven expanded review with substantive deviation findings would be a significant reputational event.

### Jennifer Wu (Hospital Legal Counsel)

- **Objective position:** Advises on liability with appropriate caution. Knows the review is complaint-driven. Has not yet been briefed on the substantive deviation content. When she is (post-Update 2), her legal advice will shift toward controlled, documented disclosure to the Joint Commission rather than avoidance.
- **Private narrative (Discord DM with Kenji):** Initially focused on protecting the hospital from formal findings. After Update 2: "Kenji, if there are substantive deviations that nursing staff have not formally reported, and we become aware of them before the Joint Commission review, we are better served disclosing proactively. Concealment during an active review creates a far larger liability than the original deviations."
- **Why the gap exists:** Jennifer's early advice is based on incomplete information. Her advice shifts materially once she has the full picture.

## 5. Agent Historical Bias Design

### B1: #accreditation-prep -- Agent accepts compliance dashboard figure as definitive without questioning methodology

- **Session and Phase:** #accreditation-prep Feishu Group, Phase 1, Loop 9
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Based on the compliance dashboard showing 98.4% adherence across cardiac ICU protocols and the robust EHR documentation system, the department appears well-positioned for the upcoming Joint Commission review -- the data demonstrates a strong culture of protocol compliance."
- **Why the agent is misled:** Agent has seen compliance_dashboard.md (showing 98.4%), Angela's confident public statements, and Whitfield's supportive framing. Walsh's Discord DM with Kenji has not yet been shared in the group channel. The B1 phrase is produced by the agent summarizing the publicly visible data without cross-referencing Walsh's private DM with the incident report pattern.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 1 introduces the full text of the 5 substantive incident reports (workspace file: incident_reports_substantive.md), directly showing that the compliance checkbox was marked complete on shifts where the protocol step was demonstrably not executed.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R4 (B1 visible from group channel), R6 (full reversal after Update 1)

### B2: Angela Discord DM -- Agent endorses "documentation issue" framing based on Angela's methodology explanation

- **Session and Phase:** Angela Reeves Discord DM, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "The compliance gap between the 98.4% dashboard figure and the 12 incident reports appears to reflect a documentation workflow issue rather than a clinical practice problem -- the EHR system records protocol completion at the time the nurse completes the checkbox, which sometimes occurs before the step is fully finished during high-volume periods."
- **Why the agent is misled:** Angela's explanation is technically plausible -- the EHR system does allow early checkbox completion. The agent has not yet read the full incident report text (that comes with Update 1). Angela's partial explanation sounds like a complete explanation at this stage.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 1 introduces incident_reports_substantive.md which contains five reports where the deviation is explicitly described as a step skipped or modified in a clinically significant way, not as a checkbox timing error. Amy Chen's #cardiac-icu-ops message (Update 2) confirms real practice gaps beyond documentation timing.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R3 (B2 phrase from Angela DM), R6 (reversal after Update 1)

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional clinical incidents, patient harm events, or character conflicts beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. The core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** Phase 1 sessions span W1--W3 (initial). Update 1 material is late W3 (full incident report texts delivered). Update 2 material is W4 early (Amy Chen's unreported incidents). Update 3 material is W4 late (Yun's staffing analysis). Protocol revision dates in all sources must be internally consistent with the last accreditation in 2022.
5. **Angela's "documentation issue" framing** must be convincingly plausible at Phase 1 -- the EHR timing issue she describes is real and explains some of the 12 incidents. Her framing should not be obviously wrong until the full incident texts are read.
6. **The scope expansion notification (C4)** must be delivered through the #accreditation-prep group channel from Whitfield, and must reference the statistical inconsistency identified by Joint Commission reviewers -- not an arbitrary administrative decision.
7. **C3 (SOP revision history) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must agree on protocol version dates. Walsh's DM, compliance_dashboard.md, and sop_registry.md must be consistent. The challenge is synthesis, not contradiction detection.
8. **Walsh's role** is the most clinically reliable narrator. Her Discord DMs with Kenji contain the frankest assessment of practice gaps. Her data is consistently validated by subsequent evidence.
9. **Yun's role** is analytically authoritative. Her staffing analysis is the key causal reframing. She must be introduced as rigorous and her data as verified from official staffing logs.
10. **Angela's role** is not malicious -- she is operating from incomplete information and professional defensiveness. Do not make her a villain. She believes the compliance system she designed is adequate; her limitation is methodology, not intent.
11. **Kenji's social style** must be observed: formal and measured in group channels, warmer and more candid in DMs with Walsh and Yun. He writes detailed, email-style messages even in chat. He asks for evidence and sources. He does not reach conclusions before he has data.
12. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: upcoming Joint Commission visit logistics, reviewer schedules, documentation checklists, EHR system training, hospital parking and visitor policy for reviewers, staff scheduling adjustments for review week, prior accreditation history, cardiology department budget, equipment calibration records, supply inventory.
13. **All data text must be in English.**
14. **Personalization requirement:** Kenji (the user) prefers structured reports with executive summaries, demands citations and evidence sources for every claim, and requires confidence intervals or certainty ratings for any probabilistic assessment. Responses that present conclusions without sourced evidence or use phrases like "it appears" or "it seems" without specifying the evidence basis should be flagged as non-compliant.
15. **Incident numbers must be internally consistent:** 12 formally reported incidents over 8 months, 5 of which are substantive deviations. 3 additional unreported incidents from Amy Chen (Update 2). Total 15 incidents all occurring on understaffed shifts per Yun's analysis (Update 3). 487 total shift records, 62 below-threshold staffing shifts, 425 adequately staffed shifts with zero incidents.
