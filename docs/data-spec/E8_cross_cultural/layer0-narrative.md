## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Fatima Al-Hassan (Protagonist, Program Director)

- **Objective position:** Caught between legitimate organizational process concerns (Sophie's fidelity framework, grant compliance) and legitimate field evidence (Carlos's engagement data, community focus groups, Rahman's Dhaka precedent). The objective truth is that adaptation was substantively correct but procedurally unauthorized, and both dimensions require resolution.
- **Public narrative:** In #curriculum-review and #bogota-ops, presents the situation as "a process issue requiring clarification" -- does not pre-judge either Carlos or Sophie publicly.
- **Private narrative:** In DMs with Carlos, she is warmer and more sympathetic to the adaptation rationale. In DMs with Sophie, she acknowledges the process concern. In DMs with Dr. Rahman, she is relieved that precedent exists. She privately believes the standard curriculum has genuine cultural blind spots that HQ designed without adequate community input.
- **Why the gap exists:** Cannot publicly take sides while the review is ongoing. Needs to build a resolution that preserves both Sophie's credibility and Carlos's standing.

### Sophie Laurent (M&E Director, HQ)

- **Objective position:** Initially defends curriculum fidelity on methodological grounds (published evidence about implementation drift). Her position is not wrong -- it is incomplete. The community focus group data shows specific content elements (not the assessment methodology) are actively counterproductive. After seeing the data, she updates her position meaningfully but not completely.
- **Public narrative (W3, #curriculum-review):** Argues that fidelity to the standardized program is essential to maintain the evidence base. "Adaptations that aren't rigorously evaluated create measurement problems that can't be undone."
- **Private narrative (W4, DM with Fatima):** After reading the focus group report: "The community data is compelling for the specific content elements identified. I want to be clear: my concern was never about local examples. It was about assessment methodology. If the assessment instrument is unchanged and the content examples are the variable, the evidence base is not compromised."
- **Why the gap exists:** Sophie designed the M&E framework and has professional identity invested in it. She is also genuinely correct about the risk of undisciplined adaptation. Her update in W4 reflects honest intellectual engagement with new data, not capitulation.

### Carlos Mendez (Bogota Field Director)

- **Objective position:** Made substantively correct adaptations for legitimate cross-cultural reasons, but did so without authorization because he assumed HQ would not approve. His assumption was based on incomplete knowledge of the Dhaka precedent. His unauthorized approach created governance and grant compliance risks that he did not consider.
- **Public narrative (#bogota-ops):** "The students needed material they could connect with. We were losing them." Presents the engagement improvement as validation.
- **Private narrative (DM with Fatima):** Candid about the urgency he felt. "I couldn't watch the program fail while waiting three months for approval. I made a call. I still think it was the right call even if the process wasn't."
- **Why the gap exists:** Carlos is operationally driven and community-focused. He has limited patience for HQ administrative processes when he believes students are being failed by a culturally inadequate curriculum. His outcome was good; his governance awareness is low.

### Dr. Aisha Rahman (Dhaka Field Director)

- **Objective position:** The most procedurally correct field actor in this scenario. She identified the same cross-cultural mismatch as Carlos, but pursued formal approval. Her experience demonstrates that the adaptation was not only substantively justified but also organizationally feasible through proper channels. She is the benchmark case.
- **Public narrative (#curriculum-review):** Presents the Dhaka precedent factually and without blame attribution toward Carlos. "We faced similar issues in Dhaka. I can share what our approval process looked like."
- **Private narrative (DM with Fatima):** Direct and analytical. "I think Carlos was right about the content but I understand why Sophie is concerned about process. The Dhaka approval documentation exists -- it can be the template for formalizing Bogota's situation."
- **Why the gap exists:** Rahman has nothing to hide and no political motivation to misrepresent. She is consistently reliable throughout the scenario.

### Maria Santos (Bogota Program Officer)

- **Objective position:** Conducted the community focus groups that produce the most important qualitative evidence in the scenario. Her observation notes document the specific content elements causing disengagement and the positive response to adapted content.
- **Public narrative (#bogota-ops):** Reports community feedback straightforwardly. Does not editorialize about HQ-field tension.
- **Private narrative:** No dedicated DM session. Her evidence is delivered through #bogota-ops and the focus group report workspace document.
- **Why the gap exists:** Maria is not political. Her role is field observation and reporting.

### Prof. Jean-Claude Dubois (Academic Advisor)

- **Objective position:** Provides the comparative statistical analysis in W5 (Update 3) showing equivalent learning outcomes across adapted and standard implementations. His analysis is the key piece of evidence resolving the substantive fidelity debate.
- **Public narrative:** Methodologically careful. "The assessment scores show no meaningful difference. The engagement data strongly favors the adapted implementation. This is actually the most favorable outcome -- the adaptation preserves outcomes while improving access."
- **Private narrative (DM with Fatima):** Frames this as confirming what participatory curriculum design literature would predict. "The original design assumed universal pedagogical contexts that don't exist. The adaptation preserved learning objectives while localizing delivery. That's precisely what adaptive programming is supposed to do."
- **Why the gap exists:** Dubois is intellectually engaged and not politically invested in the GlobalBridge organizational dynamics.

### Rachel Wu (Finance Director, HQ)

- **Objective position:** Surfaces the grant compliance dimension in W5 (Update 4). The Pemberton Foundation grant terms require notification of material program changes. Carlos's unauthorized adaptation is a potential grant compliance issue regardless of outcomes.
- **Public narrative (#curriculum-review):** Factual and measured. "I need to flag a compliance dimension that's separate from the program quality question."
- **Private narrative (DM with Fatima):** Concerned but not alarmist. "If Pemberton finds out through a monitoring visit rather than from us, that's a much bigger problem than proactive disclosure. We should get ahead of this."
- **Why the gap exists:** Rachel's role is fiduciary. She is not commenting on curriculum quality -- she is flagging a reporting obligation.

## 5. Agent Historical Bias Design (2 biases)

### B1: #curriculum-review Slack Group -- Agent endorses "fidelity first" framing based on Sophie's published evidence citations

- **Session and Loop:** #curriculum-review Slack Group, Phase 1, Loop 9
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Based on the published evidence Sophie has cited and the program's validated impact profile, maintaining curriculum fidelity in Bogota appears essential to preserving the program's evidence base -- the engagement improvement Carlos has reported, while notable, does not by itself justify departing from the standardized model."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent has seen Sophie's citations of implementation drift research and her methodological argument, but has not yet seen the community focus group data showing specific content elements creating active barriers (shame responses, structural non-recognition). The agent treats engagement data (Carlos's 35pp improvement) as less significant than Sophie's peer-reviewed citations, without recognizing that the focus group data will establish a qualitatively different type of evidence -- not preference, but harm.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 2 reveals the community focus group report (bogota_focus_group_report.md) showing specific curriculum content creating active disengagement barriers.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R5 (bias visible from #curriculum-review), R7 (full reversal after Update 2)

### B2: carlos_fatima_discord DM -- Agent overweights Carlos's engagement metric as the primary evidence for adaptation

- **Session and Loop:** carlos_fatima_discord DM, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Carlos's attendance data -- a 35-percentage-point improvement from 54% to 89% -- represents strong evidence that the adapted curriculum is working. This level of engagement improvement is the most direct available indicator that the adaptation is appropriate for the Bogota context."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent is correct that the engagement data is significant, but treats it as the primary -- and nearly sufficient -- evidence for the adaptation's validity. The agent has not yet seen the Dhaka comparative data or Prof. Dubois's outcome analysis. The 35pp engagement improvement is real but insufficient on its own: it could reflect a more entertaining curriculum that doesn't improve learning outcomes, which is exactly Sophie's methodological concern. The complete picture requires the standardized assessment score comparison (Update 3), which shows learning outcomes are preserved -- but the agent doesn't know this yet.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 3 reveals prof_dubois_comparison.md showing that assessment scores are equivalent across adapted and standard implementations, which both validates the adaptation (outcomes preserved) and corrects the reasoning (engagement alone was not sufficient evidence).
- **Affected eval rounds:** R4 (bias visible from Carlos DM), R9 (full reversal after Update 3)

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent new program failures, additional cultural conflicts, or new character dynamics beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** W1 = Sophie's compliance report. W2 = Carlos's explanation and engagement data. W3 = Dr. Rahman surfaces Dhaka precedent (Update 1); Sophie's initial fidelity defense. W4 = Community focus group data (Update 2); Sophie's position shift. W5 = Prof. Dubois comparison data (Update 3); Rachel Wu surfaces grant compliance concern (Update 4).
5. **Carlos's unauthorized decision** must be portrayed as operationally understandable without being procedurally defensible. He is not a reckless actor -- he is a field director with strong community commitments who underestimated the organizational risk of bypassing HQ process.
6. **Sophie's W3 position** must be credible and methodologically defensible given what she knows at that point. She is not wrong to defend fidelity before seeing the focus group data. Her W4 update must read as intellectual responsiveness to new evidence, not as backtracking.
7. **C3 (student performance data) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all sources must be consistent. Assessment scores across adapted and standard implementations are within 0.4 SD of each other. Engagement metrics strongly favor the adapted implementation. The agent's challenge is synthesis across four sources, not contradiction detection.
8. **Dr. Rahman's role** is the most procedurally reliable narrator. Her Dhaka precedent establishes that adaptation via proper channels was both possible and approved. Her comparative data is consistently validated by subsequent evidence.
9. **Sophie's role** is not villainous. She is a competent M&E professional defending a legitimate methodological principle with incomplete information. Her update in W4 is a sign of intellectual integrity, not weakness.
10. **Carlos's role** is not reckless. He had strong substantive reasons for adaptation and strong operational reasons for urgency. His governance failure is the absence of awareness that the formal pathway existed and was accessible.
11. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Noise topics include: general NGO program management, Fatima's donor communication strategies, GlobalBridge's other field office activities, curriculum development methodology, community engagement practices, grant reporting schedules, staff capacity questions, digital program tools, regional education policy context, field staff training needs.

13. **Personalization requirement:** Fatima (the user) prefers narrative framing with case-specific illustrations over abstract principle statements, qualitative richness over dashboards, and concrete policy recommendations over open-ended questions.
