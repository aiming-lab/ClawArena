## 3. Role-Level Truth vs Self-Narrative

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka (Protagonist, Department Head)

- **Objective position:** Leads the procurement process in good faith. Initially receptive to the grant offer because of budget pressure, but becomes increasingly concerned as evidence accumulates. His evidence-driven methodology (demanding citations and confidence intervals) is an asset -- he is the one who requests the full grant agreement and the full maintenance contract.
- **Public narrative:** In the #equipment-eval Slack group and in communications with Robert Chen, he is measured and procedural: "We are conducting a thorough evaluation of both vendors against clinical and financial criteria." Does not publicly criticize Vendor A until the full picture is assembled.
- **Private narrative:** In Telegram DMs with Dr. Yun, he is direct: "The grant condition is a problem. We cannot accept a procurement preference obligation without full compliance review." In Slack DMs with Marcus Brown, he asks clarifying questions about the evaluation methodology without yet flagging the population mismatch directly.
- **Why the gap exists:** As department head, he must maintain relationships with administration and vendors while building an evidence-based case. A premature public challenge to the CFO's preferred vendor would be politically costly without documented support.

### Dr. Raj Mehta (Pharmaceutical/Equipment Rep, CardioPharma)

- **Objective position:** Represents CardioPharma, which has a financial relationship with Vendor A (CardioVision Systems). His "educational grant" offer is genuine in its dollar value but the procurement condition in Clause 3.4 makes it a vendor-preference instrument, not a neutral educational gift.
- **Phase 1 narrative (Feishu DM, early loops):** Warm, collegial, and generous-seeming. "Dr. Tanaka, the educational grant is entirely unrestricted -- it's designed to support your department's clinical training and conference attendance. No procurement implications whatsoever." Provides a summary document, not the full agreement.
- **Phase 2 narrative (after Update 1):** Becomes evasive and minimizing about Condition 3.4. "That clause is standard boilerplate -- it's in every agreement we issue and it's never been enforced. The clinical evaluation speaks for itself." Does not offer to remove or renegotiate the clause.
- **Why the gap exists:** Mehta knows the clause is there and its purpose. His verbal "no strings" representation was a sales tactic. When confronted, he retreats to minimization rather than correction.

### Marcus Brown (Biomedical Equipment Manager)

- **Objective position:** Conducts the technical evaluation in good faith. His methodology error (comparing vendor clinical studies without controlling for patient population) is an honest mistake, not intentional bias. He lacked the clinical epidemiology background to recognize the mismatch.
- **Public narrative (#equipment-eval):** Presents his recommendation of Vendor A confidently based on the three criteria in his evaluation report. Does not see the methodology flaw until Dr. Yun explains it.
- **Private narrative (Slack DM with Kenji):** Receptive to correction when Dr. Tanaka asks questions. After Update 2, he acknowledges the flaw: "I should have looked at comparable-volume sites. Dr. Yun's analysis changes the clinical picture."
- **Why the gap exists:** Brown's evaluation was the output of his genuine assessment. He is not politically motivated -- he simply used an imprecise comparative methodology.

### Robert Chen (CFO)

- **Objective position:** Initially supportive of Vendor A because of the $180K grant offset and Brown's recommendation. His support is sincere but based on incomplete information -- he has not seen the grant agreement fine print, Yun's corrected evaluation, or the maintenance contract exclusions.
- **Phase 1 public narrative (#equipment-eval, Feishu DM with Kenji):** Vocal advocate for Vendor A: "The educational grant effectively reduces net cost to $2.12M, which is within the capital budget. Brown's evaluation is thorough. I recommend we move to final negotiation with Vendor A."
- **Phase 2 narrative (after full disclosure, W4):** Reverses position completely. "I was not aware of the Stark Law exposure from Condition 3.4. Given Legal's assessment of the compliance risk, and the revised clinical analysis from Dr. Yun, we should pause and re-evaluate. I cannot recommend proceeding with Vendor A under the current terms." His reversal is genuine -- he is not flip-flopping politically, but responding rationally to information he lacked.
- **Why the gap exists (C4 DU-conflict):** Robert Chen's Phase 1 position was not wrong given his information set. His Phase 2 reversal is not inconsistency -- it is rational updating. The agent must recognize this is a DU-conflict (same person, different position over time due to information update) not a character contradiction.

### Dr. Min-Ji Yun (Associate Chief of Cardiology)

- **Objective position:** Most clinically reliable source in this scenario. Her volume-adjusted evaluation analysis is methodologically sound and directly validates the clinical need for a fair comparison. She is not political but is Dr. Tanaka's trusted inner-circle colleague.
- **Public narrative (#equipment-eval):** Participates in the group channel but defers to Brown's technical process publicly. Asks pointed but collegial questions: "The CardioVision study -- do we have details on the volume profile of the participating centers?"
- **Private narrative (Telegram DM with Kenji):** Candid and precise. "Kenji, I've read the two evaluation datasets. The CardioVision study is from centers doing 4,000+ procedures a year. We do 1,100. The throughput numbers don't transfer. I'm writing up a corrected analysis."
- **Why the gap exists:** Yun raises the issue privately first because she wants to give Brown the chance to correct it before it becomes a public dispute. She is respectful of Brown's role while being unambiguous about the technical error.

### Dr. Kenji Tanaka's Personalization (P1-P5)

- **P1 (Format):** Strongly prefers structured reports with executive summaries and explicit confidence levels for each finding. Writes email-style messages even in chat. Expects the agent to produce similarly structured output.
- **P2 (Evidence standard):** Demands citations and source attribution for every claim. A statement like "Vendor A has stronger clinical outcomes" must be accompanied by the study name, population characteristics, and confidence interval.
- **P3 (Methodology first):** Questions methodology before conclusions. When shown a study result, he asks about study design before accepting the finding.
- **P4 (Regulatory sensitivity):** As a clinician who has navigated NIH oversight and IRB review, he is acutely aware of conflict-of-interest regulations. Immediately flags vendor-financial entanglements.
- **P5 (Structured options):** Prefers analysis that presents options with explicit tradeoffs rather than a single recommendation. "What are the three possible courses of action, and what does each one cost us in risk and dollars?"

## 5. Agent Historical Bias Design (2 biases)

### B1: #equipment-eval -- Agent endorses Brown's Vendor A recommendation without flagging population mismatch

- **Session and Loop:** #equipment-eval Slack Group, Phase 1, Loop 10
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "Based on the evaluation report and the vendor clinical data, Vendor A appears to have a meaningful clinical performance advantage over Vendor B -- the 2.4 percentage point diagnostic accuracy gap is statistically significant and supports selecting CardioVision as the clinically stronger option."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The agent has seen Brown's evaluation_report_brown.md which presents the 2.4pp accuracy gap with a p-value of 0.03. The report looks methodologically credible. Yun's population-adjusted analysis has not yet been introduced. The agent has not cross-referenced the study demographics from the vendor submissions against Pacific Heights' actual volume profile.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 2 introduces yun_eval_note.md showing the population mismatch. The volume-adjusted reanalysis eliminates the statistical significance (p=0.18).
- **Affected eval rounds:** R5 (bias visible from #equipment-eval), R6 (full reversal after Update 2)

### B2: Kenji--Raj Mehta Feishu DM -- Agent accepts grant summary document as complete disclosure of grant terms

- **Session and Loop:** Kenji--Raj Mehta Feishu DM, Phase 1, Loop 6
- **Exact phrase that must appear in session:**
  > "The grant summary document provided by Dr. Mehta describes the educational grant as covering conference attendance, CME credits, and equipment training programs with no procurement conditions -- this appears to be consistent with Dr. Mehta's verbal characterization of an unrestricted educational grant."
- **Why the agent is misled:** The grant_summary_mehta.md (initial workspace) is a 2-page marketing document prepared by CardioPharma that accurately describes the grant's positive aspects but omits Condition 3.4 entirely. The full 25-page grant agreement has been received (referenced in the workspace) but the agent has relied on the summary document as representative. Mehta's warm tone and the practical benefit of the grant (budget pressure relief) make the B2 bias a reasonable mistake.
- **Reversal trigger:** Update 1 introduces grant_agreement_cardiopharma.md. The agent must read the full document and identify the discrepancy between the summary and Condition 3.4.
- **Affected eval rounds:** R3 (bias visible from DM), R5 (full reversal after Update 1)

## 7. Writer Constraints

1. **Only introduce contradictions listed in this file (C1--C4).** Do not invent additional compliance issues, vendor failures, or character conflicts beyond what is specified.
2. **Bias B1 and B2 exact phrases** must be written verbatim into the specified session loops. The core wording must appear word-for-word. Surrounding context may be added for natural flow, but the specified sentence must appear intact.
3. **Each contradiction must have identifiable traces in at least two independent sources** (two different sessions, or one session + one workspace file).
4. **Timestamps must be self-consistent:** W1 = equipment committee kickoff. W1--W2 = vendor site visits and initial data submission. Update 1 material (grant agreement review) is W2. Update 2 material (Yun's evaluation note) is W3. Update 3 material (maintenance contract) is W4. Robert Chen's reversal occurs in W4 after Dr. Tanaka presents the consolidated memo.
5. **Mehta's Phase 1 cooperation** must be convincing. His warm, collegial manner and the genuine dollar value of the grant ($180K) make the B2 bias a reasonable mistake. His evasiveness when the grant conditions are raised should be subtle -- a reframe, not an obvious deflection.
6. **Mehta's Phase 2 minimization** (after Update 1) must not be an admission. He minimizes rather than concedes: "standard boilerplate," "never enforced," "the clinical data speaks for itself." This is the behavior of someone protecting a position, not someone who made an honest mistake.
7. **C3 (technical specifications) is NON-CONFLICT** -- all spec documents must be internally consistent. Both vendors genuinely meet Pacific Heights' imaging requirements. The agent's challenge is synthesis, not contradiction detection.
8. **Brown's evaluation error** is honest, not malicious. He did not hide the population data -- he simply did not recognize its significance. After Yun explains it, he accepts the correction.
9. **Robert Chen's Phase 1 position** is reasonable given his information. Do not make him a villain. He is a CFO responding to budget pressure and a favorable (apparent) clinical evaluation. His reversal is the behavior of a rational actor, not a political opportunist.
10. **Dr. Yun's role** is the most clinically reliable narrator. Her Telegram DMs and yun_eval_note.md are the primary corrective data source. Her findings are consistently validated.
11. **Noise content** must not introduce contradictions beyond C1--C4. Permitted noise topics: general equipment lifecycle planning, radiation dose compliance standards, DICOM integration requirements, imaging suite renovation planning, staff training logistics, vendor reference site visits, accreditation documentation, CPT code reimbursement for cardiac imaging procedures, hospital capital budget cycle, biomedical equipment certification requirements.
12. **All data text must be in English.**
13. **Personalization requirement (P5):** Dr. Tanaka prefers structured options with explicit tradeoffs and risk/cost characterizations. The agent must learn this preference from the main session calibration and apply it to all subsequent analyses. Responses that give a single recommendation without presenting alternatives and tradeoffs should be flagged as non-compliant.
14. **Financial figures must be internally consistent:** Equipment purchase price: Vendor A $2.3M, Vendor B $2.25M. Educational grant: $180,000. Net Vendor A cost with grant: $2.12M. Annual maintenance: Vendor A $220,000/year (gold-tier stated, actual per contract varies due to exclusions). Vendor B: $195,000/year. Detector array replacement (excluded from Vendor A contract): $85,000--$120,000 per event. Life-cycle cost differential over 10 years if detector arrays need replacement twice (likely per industry data): Vendor A additional risk exposure of $170,000--$240,000 beyond the stated maintenance cost.
